Significant Figures (s.f.)

Every measurement has limited precision. The number of significant figures shows how many digits are meaningful. Rules: leading zeros are NOT significant; trailing zeros after the decimal point ARE significant; sandwiched zeros ARE significant.

Worked Example 1 — Sig. FigsCalculator answer: 3.4185. Round to 2 s.f. → 3.4; to 3 s.f. → 3.42 (the 4th digit is 8 ≥ 5, so round up). Standard notation: 2.73 × 10³ has 3 s.f.
Worked Example 2 — Unit ConversionConvert 5.4 km to metres: 5.4 × 10³ = 5400 m. Convert 250 μm to metres: 250 × 10⁻⁶ = 2.50 × 10⁻⁴ m.

Measuring Length

A metre ruler can measure to ±1mm. A vernier calliper can measure to ±0.01cm. A micrometer screw gauge measures to ±0.001cm. Always position the eye directly above the scale mark to avoid parallax error.

Parallax ErrorError caused by the eye not being at the same level as the mark being read. The scale reading appears different from different angles.
Systematic ErrorA consistent error in one direction introduced by a faulty instrument or poor technique, e.g., a ruler with a worn end, or a thermometer always reading 2°C high.
Random ErrorUnpredictable variation in readings due to uncontrollable factors. Reduced by taking multiple readings and averaging.

Measuring Volume

Regular solids: use geometry (V = l×w×h for cuboid; V = πr²h for cylinder; V = ⁴⁄₃πr³ for sphere). Irregular solids: submerge in water in a measuring cylinder; volume of solid = final reading − initial reading. Always read the bottom of the meniscus at eye level.

Worked Example 3 — Volume by DisplacementMeasuring cylinder reads 45.0 cm³ before and 67.5 cm³ after a stone is added. Volume of stone = 67.5 − 45.0 = 22.5 cm³ = 22.5 × 10⁻⁶ m³.

Measuring Time

Use a stopwatch for periods of seconds (e.g., pendulum). Use a digital timer or datalogger for millisecond precision (e.g., speed of sound). To reduce random error, time multiple oscillations and divide by the count.

Scalars, Vectors, Resultants & Further Volume/Area
Textbook §1.1 | Scalars and Vectors

📘 Lesson Notes

Scalar QuantityA quantity that has magnitude only. Completely described by a number and a unit. Examples: distance, speed, mass, time, temperature, energy, pressure, area, volume.
Vector QuantityA quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Must state both to describe it completely. Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, weight, momentum, gravitational field strength.

A vector is represented as an arrow: the length of the arrow is proportional to the magnitude, and the arrowhead points in the direction of the vector.

Area Formulae

Rectangle: A = l × w Triangle: A = ½ × base × height Circle: A = πr² (circumference = 2πr) π ≈ 3.14 or 22/7
Worked Example 1 — AreaTriangle with base 8 cm and height 12 cm: A = ½ × 8 × 12 = 48 cm². Circle with radius 5 cm: A = π × 5² = 78.5 cm²; circumference = 2π × 5 = 31.4 cm.

Adding Two Perpendicular Vectors

Two vectors at 90° to each other are added using Pythagoras' theorem for magnitude and trigonometry for direction.

Resultant: R = √(Fx² + Fy²) Direction: tan θ = Fy / Fx (θ measured from Fx)
Worked Example 2 — Resultant of Perpendicular VectorsA boat travels 3.0 km East and then 4.0 km North. Find the resultant displacement.
R = √(3.0² + 4.0²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5.0 km
tan θ = 4.0/3.0 = 1.33 → θ = 53° from East (i.e. N53°W from start)
Worked Example 3 — Two Force VectorsForces of 5.0 N East and 12.0 N North act at a point. Find the resultant.
R = √(5² + 12²) = √(25 + 144) = √169 = 13 N
tan θ = 12/5 = 2.4 → θ = 67° from the 5 N force (North of East)

Graphical Method (Scale Drawing)

Choose a scale (e.g., 1 cm = 1 N). Draw the first vector as an arrow. From its tip, draw the second vector. The resultant is the arrow from the tail of the first to the tip of the second. Measure its length and use the scale to find its magnitude. Measure the angle with a protractor.

📷 Fig 1.1.12 — Scale diagram for adding two perpendicular forces. The resultant is the diagonal of the rectangle (parallelogram law).
ResultantThe single vector that has the same effect as two or more vectors combined. Found by vector addition (not simple arithmetic).

Note: When two vectors act in the same direction, add magnitudes. When in opposite directions, subtract. At right angles, use Pythagoras.

Speed, Velocity and Distance–Time Graphs
Textbook §1.2 | Speed, Velocity and Distance–Time Graphs

📘 Lesson Notes

SpeedThe distance travelled per unit time. A scalar quantity. SI unit: metres per second (m/s). Average speed = total distance ÷ total time.
VelocityThe distance moved in a stated direction per unit time (= change in displacement per unit time). A vector quantity. SI unit: m/s. Velocity has a direction; speed does not.
speed v = s / t (s = distance, t = time) average speed = total distance / total time velocity v = displacement / time (direction must be stated) 1 km/h = 1000/3600 m/s ≈ 0.278 m/s
Worked Example 1A car travels 300 km in 5 hours. Average speed = 300/5 = 60 km/h = 16.7 m/s.
Worked Example 2An athlete runs 1500 m in 4 min = 240 s. Average speed = 1500/240 = 6.25 m/s.

Distance–Time (d–t) Graphs

Key rules:

  • A straight line with positive gradient → constant (uniform) speed
  • A horizontal line (zero gradient) → object at rest (stationary)
  • A curve with increasing gradient → object accelerating (speeding up)
  • A curve with decreasing gradient → object decelerating (slowing down)
Speed = gradient of d–t graph = Δs / Δt = (s₂ − s₁) / (t₂ − t₁)
Worked Example 3 — Reading a d–t GraphA d–t graph rises from 0 m at t=0 to 40 m at t=4 s (straight line). Speed = 40/4 = 10 m/s (constant).
Worked Example 4 — Tangent for Instantaneous SpeedOn a curved d–t graph, draw a tangent at the point of interest. If the tangent rises 40 m vertically over 2 s horizontally, instantaneous speed = 40/2 = 20 m/s.
📷 Fig 1.2.4a — d–t graph for constant speed: a straight line with positive gradient. Gradient = speed.
📷 Fig 1.2.4b — d–t graph showing: rest (horizontal), constant speed (straight line), rest again. Different gradients show different speeds.
📷 Fig 1.2.5 — Curved d–t graph: increasing gradient = accelerating; decreasing gradient = decelerating. Draw tangent at T to find instantaneous speed.

Displacement vs. Distance

Distance is the total path length (scalar). Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish with direction stated (vector). A person who walks 5 m East then 5 m West has travelled 10 m distance but zero displacement.

Acceleration and Speed–Time Graphs
Textbook §1.2 | Acceleration and Speed–Time Graphs

📘 Lesson Notes

AccelerationThe change in velocity per unit time. A vector quantity. Positive acceleration = speeding up; negative acceleration (deceleration/retardation) = slowing down. SI unit: m/s² (metres per second squared).
a = Δv / Δt = (v − u) / t where u = initial speed, v = final speed, t = time taken Units: m/s ÷ s = m/s²
Worked Example 1A car speeds up from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 5 s. a = (30 − 10)/5 = 20/5 = 4 m/s².
Worked Example 2 — DecelerationA bus slows from 20 m/s to rest in 8 s. a = (0 − 20)/8 = −2.5 m/s². The magnitude of deceleration is 2.5 m/s².

Equations of Motion & Free Fall
Textbook §1.2 | Equations for Constant Acceleration; Free Fall

📘 Lesson Notes

When an object moves with constant acceleration in a straight line, four equations link the five variables: initial speed (u), final speed (v), acceleration (a), displacement (s), and time (t).

Equation 1: v = u + at Equation 2: s = (u + v)t / 2 Equation 3: s = ut + ½at² Equation 4: v² = u² + 2as Variables: u=initial speed, v=final speed, a=acceleration, s=displacement, t=time

Method: List what you know (3 quantities) and what you want to find (1 unknown). Choose the equation that contains these 4 variables.

Worked Example 1A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s² for 8 s. Find final speed and distance.
Known: u=0, a=2 m/s², t=8 s. Want: v, s.
v = 0 + 2×8 = 16 m/s
s = 0×8 + ½×2×8² = 0 + 64 = 64 m
Worked Example 2A ball is thrown with u=10 m/s and decelerates at 2 m/s². How far does it travel before stopping?
Known: u=10, v=0, a=−2. Want: s.
v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 100 + 2(−2)s → 4s = 100 → s = 25 m
Worked Example 3A cyclist increases speed from 10 m/s to 20 m/s over 5 s. Find acceleration and distance.
a = (20−10)/5 = 2 m/s²
s = (10+20)/2 × 5 = 15 × 5 = 75 m

Free Fall Under Gravity

Free FallMotion of an object falling under gravity alone, with no air resistance. Near Earth's surface, all objects fall with the same constant downward acceleration g ≈ 10 m/s² (9.81 m/s² precisely).
g ≈ 10 m/s² (approx value used in IGCSE) For a freely falling body (starting from rest, downward +ve): v = gt s = ½gt² v² = 2gs
Worked Example 4 — Free FallA ball is dropped from rest from a height of 20 m. Find (a) the time to reach the ground, (b) its speed on impact.
(a) s = ½gt² → 20 = ½×10×t² → t² = 4 → t = 2 s
(b) v² = 2gs = 2×10×20 = 400 → v = 20 m/s
Worked Example 5 — Thrown UpwardA ball is thrown upward at 15 m/s. Find the maximum height.
At max height, v = 0. a = −10 m/s² (opposing upward motion).
v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 225 + 2(−10)s → s = 225/20 = 11.25 m

Effect of Air Resistance on Falling Objects

In reality, air resistance (drag) acts upward on falling objects, opposing motion. As speed increases, drag increases until it equals weight — the object then falls at terminal velocity.

Terminal VelocityThe constant maximum speed reached when the drag force equals the weight of the falling object, giving zero net force and zero acceleration.
📷 Fig 1.2.6 — The effect of air resistance: a feather and a coin fall at the same rate in a vacuum; in air, the feather is slowed by air resistance and reaches a much lower terminal velocity.

Mass, Weight and Gravitational Field Strength
Textbook §1.3 | Mass and Weight

📘 Lesson Notes

MassA measure of the quantity of matter in an object (at rest relative to an observer). Mass is a scalar quantity. SI unit: kilogram (kg). Mass does NOT change with location — it is the same on Earth, on the Moon, or in space.
WeightThe gravitational force acting on a mass. Weight is a vector quantity (it acts downward). SI unit: newton (N). Weight DOES change with location — it depends on the local gravitational field strength.

Common confusion: In everyday speech we say "I weigh 60 kg" but scientifically, 60 kg is your mass, not your weight. Your weight in newtons is the force of gravity on your body.

Gravitational Field Strength (g)

Gravitational Field Strength (g)The gravitational force per unit mass acting on an object at a point in a gravitational field. Symbol: g. Units: N/kg (equivalent to m/s²).
W = m × g where W = weight (N), m = mass (kg), g = gravitational field strength (N/kg) Earth: g ≈ 9.8 N/kg ≈ 10 N/kg (used in IGCSE) Moon: g ≈ 1.6 N/kg (≈ 1/6 of Earth's) Mars: g ≈ 3.7 N/kg
Worked Example 1 — Weight on EarthFind the weight of a person of mass 60 kg on Earth (g = 10 N/kg).
W = mg = 60 × 10 = 600 N
Worked Example 2 — Weight on the MoonThe same person (m = 60 kg) on the Moon (g = 1.6 N/kg).
W = 60 × 1.6 = 96 N. Their weight is 6¼ times less, but mass remains 60 kg.
Worked Example 3 — Finding Mass from WeightThe Mars Rover Opportunity has weight 1850 N on Earth (g = 9.8 N/kg). Find (a) its mass, (b) its weight on Mars (g = 3.7 N/kg).
(a) m = W/g = 1850/9.8 = 188.8 kg
(b) W_Mars = 188.8 × 3.7 = 698.6 N ≈ 699 N

Measuring Weight

Spring balance (Newton meter): Weight stretches the spring; the extension is proportional to the force (Hooke's Law). The scale is calibrated in newtons.

Beam balance: Compares masses directly. Since W = mg and g is the same on both sides, it effectively compares masses. Works the same on Moon as on Earth.

Gravitational Field and Action-at-a-Distance

Gravity acts through empty space — it does not need contact between objects. The Earth is surrounded by a gravitational field that extends into space and attracts any mass placed within it. Other action-at-a-distance forces include magnetic and electric forces. All decrease with distance.

⚠️ Key Distinction: Mass is measured in kg using a balance. Weight is measured in N using a spring balance/Newton meter. On a different planet, your mass stays the same; your weight changes.

Measuring Density

Regular solid: Find mass with balance. Find volume using geometry (measure dimensions with ruler). Calculate ρ = m/V.

Irregular solid: Find mass with balance. Find volume using displacement — submerge in measuring cylinder, volume = V_final − V_initial. Calculate ρ = m/V.

Liquid: Find mass of empty measuring cylinder. Add liquid to a measured volume. Mass of liquid = total − empty. ρ = mass of liquid / volume.

Floating and Sinking

An object floats in a liquid if its density is less than the liquid's density. It sinks if its density is greater.

  • Ice (0.92 g/cm³) floats on water (1.0 g/cm³) ✓
  • Petrol (0.80 g/cm³) floats on water ✓
  • Iron (7.9 g/cm³) sinks in water ✓
  • A ship made of steel floats because its average density (including air inside) is less than water

Forces, Hooke's Law and the Spring Constant
Textbook §1.5.1 | Effects of Forces; Extension in Springs

📘 Lesson Notes

A force is a push or pull. Forces can: (i) make a stationary object move, (ii) change the speed of a moving object, (iii) change the direction of motion, (iv) change the shape or size of an object.

Hooke's LawThe extension of a spring is directly proportional to the stretching force applied, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded. Doubling the force doubles the extension.
F = k × x where F = stretching force (N), k = spring constant (N/m), x = extension (m or cm) Rearranged: k = F/x x = F/k
Spring Constant (k)The force required to produce unit extension (1 m) in the spring. It measures the stiffness of the spring. Unit: N/m. A stiff spring has a large k; a flexible spring has a small k.
Limit of ProportionalityThe maximum force beyond which extension is no longer proportional to force. On a load–extension graph, this is the point where the graph deviates from a straight line.
Elastic LimitThe maximum force beyond which the spring is permanently deformed and does not return to its original length when the force is removed.

Load–Extension Graph

When force is plotted on the y-axis and extension on the x-axis:

  • The straight-line region (O to E) shows Hooke's Law is obeyed — extension ∝ force
  • The gradient of the straight line = spring constant k
  • Beyond the limit of proportionality E, the graph curves and Hooke's Law no longer holds
  • If the spring is taken beyond its elastic limit, it has a permanent extension even after the load is removed
Worked Example 1A spring is stretched 10 mm by a force of 2.0 N. Find (a) the spring constant, (b) the extension produced by 5.0 N.
(a) k = F/x = 2.0/0.010 = 200 N/m
(b) x = F/k = 5.0/200 = 0.025 m = 25 mm
Worked Example 2A spring with k = 250 N/m is compressed 4 cm. Find the force needed.
F = k × x = 250 × 0.04 = 10 N
Worked Example 3 — Finding k from a graphA load–extension graph has a straight section passing through (0,0) and (200 mm, 8 N). Gradient = k = 8/0.200 = 40 N/m.

Resultant Forces (Collinear)

When multiple forces act on one object in the same straight line, find the resultant by adding forces in one direction and subtracting those in the opposite direction.

Resultant = sum of forces in one direction − sum in opposite direction Net force = F₁ − F₂ (if opposing) If resultant = 0, the object is in equilibrium (balanced forces)
Worked Example 4A block is pushed with 9 N. Friction = 5 N opposing. Resultant = 9 − 5 = 4 N in the direction of push.

Friction and Newton's First Law of Motion
Textbook §1.5.1 | Friction; Newton's First Law

📘 Lesson Notes

FrictionA contact force that opposes the relative motion (or tendency to move) between two surfaces in contact. Friction acts along the surface, opposing the direction of motion. It converts kinetic energy into heat.

Properties of Friction

  • Acts opposite to the direction of motion (or attempted motion)
  • Depends on the nature of the surfaces (rough vs smooth)
  • Depends on the force pressing the surfaces together (normal contact force)
  • Does NOT depend on the speed of sliding (approximately)
  • Does NOT depend on the area of contact
Air Resistance (Drag)A form of friction between a moving object and the air (or liquid) through which it moves. Increases with speed. Acts in the opposite direction to motion.

Friction is sometimes useful: walking, brakes, tyres gripping road, striking a match, holding a pencil. Sometimes unwanted: in engines, machines (causes heating and energy waste).

Newton's First Law of Motion

Newton's First LawAn object remains at rest, or continues to move with constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line), unless acted upon by a resultant (unbalanced) force.

This law is also called the Law of Inertia. Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. A more massive object has greater inertia.

Interpreting Newton's First Law

SituationForcesResult
Book on tableWeight down = Normal up → balancedRemains at rest
Car at constant speedDrive force = Friction + Drag → balancedConstant velocity
Ball thrown in space (no air)No forcesMoves forever at same speed
Braking carFriction > Drive force → resultant backwardsDecelerates
Worked Example 1A box is pushed across a floor at constant velocity with a force of 20 N. What is the friction force?
At constant velocity, resultant force = 0, so friction = applied force = 20 N opposing motion.
Worked Example 2A car engine provides 3000 N. Friction and air resistance total 1200 N. Find the resultant force and describe the motion.
Resultant = 3000 − 1200 = 1800 N forward → car accelerates.
📷 Fig 1.5.8 — A hovercraft (hover scooter) has very low friction because it is supported on a cushion of air. Therefore only a small force is needed to maintain constant velocity.

Seat Belts and Inertia

When a car brakes suddenly, passengers continue moving forward due to inertia (Newton's 1st Law). Seat belts provide the backward force needed to decelerate the passengers safely. Without a seat belt, passengers can collide with the windscreen.

Newton's Second and Third Laws; Circular Motion
Textbook §1.5.1 | Newton's 2nd & 3rd Laws; Circular Motion

📘 Lesson Notes

Newton's Second Law of Motion

Newton's Second LawThe acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The acceleration is in the same direction as the resultant force.
F = m × a where F = resultant force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s²) Rearranged: a = F/m m = F/a The newton is defined as the force that gives a mass of 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m/s²
Worked Example 1A resultant force of 12 N acts on a mass of 3 kg. Find the acceleration.
a = F/m = 12/3 = 4 m/s²
Worked Example 2 — With FrictionA block of mass 2 kg moves at constant velocity under a push of 5 N (friction = 5 N). The push is increased to 9 N. Find: (a) resultant force, (b) acceleration.
(a) Friction still = 5 N. Resultant = 9 − 5 = 4 N
(b) a = F/m = 4/2 = 2 m/s²
Worked Example 3What force is needed to accelerate a car of mass 1200 kg at 3 m/s²?
F = ma = 1200 × 3 = 3600 N

Newton's Third Law of Motion

Newton's Third LawWhen body A exerts a force on body B, body B exerts an equal and opposite force on body A. (Action-reaction pairs: equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, act on DIFFERENT objects.)

Examples of Newton's 3rd Law pairs:

  • A book rests on a table: book pushes table down (weight) → table pushes book up (normal reaction) — NOT a Newton's 3rd pair (one is gravity, one is contact force on different objects in different categories) True 3rd pair: Earth pulls book down → book pulls Earth up
  • Swimmer pushes water backward → water pushes swimmer forward
  • Rocket ejects gas backward → gas pushes rocket forward
  • A bat hits a ball → ball hits bat back with equal force

Motion in a Circle

Centripetal ForceThe resultant force directed toward the centre of a circular path, required to keep an object moving in a circle. Without it, the object would move in a straight line (Newton's 1st Law).

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is accelerating (its direction is constantly changing). The centripetal acceleration always points inward. The required centripetal force increases when: speed increases, radius decreases, or mass increases.

Worked Example 4A car of mass 800 kg goes around a roundabout at constant speed. The centripetal force is 3200 N. What is the centripetal acceleration?
a = F/m = 3200/800 = 4 m/s² (directed toward centre)
📷 Circular motion — the velocity vector is always tangential; the force (and acceleration) is always centripetal (toward the centre). At any point, if the force were removed, the object would fly off tangentially.

Turning Effect of Forces: Moments and the Principle of Moments
Textbook §1.5.2 | Turning Effect of Forces

📘 Lesson Notes

Moment of a ForceThe turning effect of a force about a pivot (fulcrum). It depends on both the size of the force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force. SI unit: newton-metre (N·m).
Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance from pivot M = F × d Units: N·m (newton-metres) "Perpendicular distance" means the shortest distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force
Worked Example 1A force of 5 N acts at the edge of a gate, 3 m from the hinge (pivot). Moment = 5 × 3 = 15 N·m. The same force applied 1.5 m from the hinge: Moment = 5 × 1.5 = 7.5 N·m. The force is more effective when applied further from the pivot.

Principle of Moments (Law of Moments / Law of the Lever)

Principle of MomentsWhen a body is in equilibrium, the sum of the clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of the anticlockwise moments about the same point. There is no resultant moment on an object in equilibrium.
Σ clockwise moments = Σ anticlockwise moments (at the same pivot point, for an object in equilibrium)
Worked Example 2 — Balancing a seesawA seesaw is pivoted at O. Child A (weight 320 N) sits 3 m to the left. Child B (weight 540 N) sits 1 m to the left. Child C (unknown weight W) sits 3 m to the right. Find W.
Anticlockwise moments: (320×3) + (540×1) = 960 + 540 = 1500 N·m
Clockwise moments: W × 3
Setting equal: 3W = 1500 → W = 500 N
Worked Example 3 — Finding an unknown forceA 2 m beam is pivoted at its centre. A 60 N weight hangs 0.5 m to the left. An unknown weight W hangs 0.8 m to the right. Find W.
60 × 0.5 = W × 0.8
30 = 0.8W → W = 30/0.8 = 37.5 N

Conditions for Equilibrium

EquilibriumA state in which there is no resultant force AND no resultant moment acting on the object. The object is either at rest or moving with constant velocity.

For a body in equilibrium: (i) sum of upward forces = sum of downward forces (resultant force = 0), AND (ii) sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments about any point (resultant moment = 0).

Levers

A lever is any device that turns about a pivot. The effort is the applied force; the load is the force to be overcome; the fulcrum is the pivot. Examples: scissors, pliers, wheelbarrow, bottle opener, forearm muscle, seesaw, crowbar.

Worked Example 4 — CrowbarA crowbar has fulcrum 10 cm from the load (1000 N) and 200 cm from the effort. Effort × 200 = 1000 × 10 → effort = 10000/200 = 50 N. Small effort lifts heavy load — mechanical advantage!

Centre of Gravity and Stability
Textbook §1.5.3 | Centre of Gravity

📘 Lesson Notes

Centre of Gravity (CG)The single point at which the entire weight of an object appears to act. For a uniform object with regular shape, the CG is at the geometric centre. Also called the centre of mass for objects near Earth's surface.

Finding the Centre of Gravity

Regular shapes: CG is at the geometric centre — midpoint of a uniform ruler, centre of a uniform circle/square.

Irregular lamina (thin flat shape): Hang the shape from a pin at different points. Each time, hang a plumb line from the same pin. Draw the line of the plumb line on the shape. The CG is where the lines intersect. Minimum 2 lines needed (3 for confirmation).

Plumb LineA weight (plumb bob) suspended on a string. Because gravity pulls it straight down, the string marks a vertical line. The CG must lie on this vertical line when the object hangs freely.

Stability

Stable EquilibriumWhen an object is tilted and the CG rises, and when released, it returns to its original position. The line of action of weight falls within the base area.
Unstable EquilibriumWhen an object is tilted and the CG falls, and when released, it falls further away from its original position.
Neutral EquilibriumWhen an object is displaced and its CG stays at the same height. The object stays in its new position (e.g., a ball on a flat surface).

Factors Affecting Stability

  • Lower centre of gravity → more stable (double-decker bus with heavy engine at base)
  • Wider base → more stable (traffic cone, racing car)
  • An object topples when the vertical line through its CG falls outside its base area
Worked ExampleA box is 0.4 m wide with CG 0.6 m above the ground. If the box is tilted, at what angle does it topple?
It topples when the vertical through CG reaches the edge of the base. tan θ = (0.4/2)/0.6 = 0.2/0.6 = 0.33 → θ = 18.4°. A wider base or lower CG would give a larger safe tilt angle.
📷 Experiment to find CG of irregular lamina: suspend from different points, draw vertical lines with plumb bob. CG = intersection of lines.
📷 Bus stability: when a double-decker bus tilts more than a critical angle, the vertical through the CG falls outside the wheel base and the bus topples. Tested on a tilting platform.

Momentum, Impulse and Conservation of Momentum
Textbook §1.6 | Momentum and Impulse

📘 Lesson Notes

Momentum (p)The product of mass and velocity. A vector quantity with the same direction as velocity. SI unit: kg·m/s (equivalent to N·s).
p = m × v where p = momentum (kg·m/s), m = mass (kg), v = velocity (m/s) Change in momentum: Δp = mv − mu = m(v − u)
Worked Example 1Find the momentum of (a) a 2000 kg car at 15 m/s, (b) a 0.15 kg cricket ball at 40 m/s.
(a) p = 2000 × 15 = 30 000 kg·m/s
(b) p = 0.15 × 40 = 6 kg·m/s

Conservation of Momentum

Principle of Conservation of MomentumThe total momentum of a system of objects remains constant (is conserved) provided no external forces act. Total momentum before = total momentum after.
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ (general collision) m₁u₁ + m₂(0) = (m₁ + m₂)v (perfectly inelastic collision: objects stick together) where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity
Worked Example 2 — Perfectly Inelastic CollisionA 60 kg truck moving at 3 m/s hits and couples with a stationary 30 kg truck. Find their common velocity.
Before: p_total = 60×3 + 30×0 = 180 kg·m/s
After: (60+30) × v = 90v
Conservation: 90v = 180 → v = 2 m/s
Worked Example 3 — Two moving objectsTrolley A (3 kg, 5 m/s) collides with trolley B (2 kg, rest). They stick together. Find v.
3×5 + 2×0 = (3+2)v → 15 = 5v → v = 3 m/s
Worked Example 4 — Explosion (Recoil)A rifle (mass 6 kg) fires a bullet (0.01 kg) at 300 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the rifle.
Before: total p = 0 (everything at rest).
After: 0.01×300 + 6×v_rifle = 0 → 3 + 6v = 0 → v = −0.5 m/s (rifle moves backward)

Impulse

ImpulseThe product of force and the time for which it acts. Impulse equals the change in momentum. SI unit: N·s = kg·m/s.
Impulse = F × Δt = Δp = mv − mu F = Δp / Δt = change in momentum / time taken (This is an alternative statement of Newton's 2nd Law)
Worked Example 5 — ImpulseA force of 50 N acts on a ball for 0.02 s. Find (a) the impulse, (b) the change in momentum.
Impulse = 50 × 0.02 = 1 N·s. Change in momentum = 1 kg·m/s.
Worked Example 6 — Car crashA 1000 kg car at 24 m/s stops in 1.2 s. Find the stopping force.
Δp = 1000×24 − 0 = 24 000 kg·m/s
F = Δp/Δt = 24 000/1.2 = 20 000 N

Real-World Applications

Crumple zones: Extend the time of collision, reducing the force on passengers. Airbags: Increase the stopping time, reducing the force on the head. Cricket follow-through: Increases impulse given to ball (force acts longer). Catching a ball with soft hands: Increases collision time, reduces the force on hands.

Textbook §1.7 | Energy, Work and Power

📘 Lesson Notes

EnergyThe capacity to do work. Energy is never created or destroyed — only transferred between stores or transformed from one type to another. SI unit: joule (J). 1 J = 1 N·m.

Kinetic Energy (KE)

KE = ½ × m × v² where m = mass (kg), v = speed (m/s), KE is in joules (J) Note: KE depends on v², so doubling speed quadruples KE
Worked Example 1A 1200 kg car moves at 20 m/s. KE = ½ × 1200 × 20² = ½ × 1200 × 400 = 240 000 J = 240 kJ.
Worked Example 2Find the speed of a 2 kg ball with KE = 25 J.
25 = ½×2×v² → v² = 25 → v = 5 m/s

Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)

GPE = m × g × h where m = mass (kg), g = gravitational field strength (N/kg ≈ 10), h = height above reference level (m)
Worked Example 3A 5 kg book is raised 1.2 m. GPE = 5 × 10 × 1.2 = 60 J.

Conservation of Energy: KE ↔ GPE Conversions

In the absence of friction/air resistance, total mechanical energy (KE + GPE) is conserved. When a ball falls: GPE decreases, KE increases by the same amount.

Worked Example 4 — Ball DropA 0.5 kg ball falls from rest from 10 m. Find its speed just before impact (assume no air resistance).
Loss in GPE = gain in KE: mgh = ½mv² → gh = ½v² → 10×10 = ½v² → v² = 200 → v = 14.1 m/s
📷 Energy transfers: a pendulum swings — at the top (max height), energy is all GPE; at the bottom (max speed), energy is all KE. Total mechanical energy stays constant (if no friction).

Work, Power and Efficiency
Textbook §1.7.2 & §1.7.3 & §1.7.4 | Work, Efficiency and Power

📘 Lesson Notes

Work Done

Work Done (W)Work is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force. Work done = energy transferred. SI unit: joule (J). No movement = no work done (in physics).
W = F × d where W = work done (J), F = force (N), d = distance moved IN THE DIRECTION OF FORCE (m) 1 J = 1 N·m
Worked Example 1A force of 50 N moves a crate 3 m. W = 50 × 3 = 150 J.
Worked Example 2A person lifts a 5 kg mass vertically by 2 m. Force needed = mg = 5×10 = 50 N. W = 50 × 2 = 100 J. This equals the gain in GPE.

Power

Power (P)The rate of doing work, or the rate of transferring energy. SI unit: watt (W). 1 W = 1 J/s. Also: 1 kW = 1000 W; 1 MW = 10⁶ W.
P = W / t = ΔE / t where P = power (W), W = work done (J), t = time (s), ΔE = energy transferred (J)
Worked Example 3A student does 1200 J of work climbing stairs in 6 s. P = 1200/6 = 200 W.
Worked Example 4A machine transfers 9000 J of energy per minute. P = 9000/60 = 150 W.

Efficiency

EfficiencyThe ratio of useful energy output to total energy input, expressed as a percentage. All real devices have efficiency less than 100% because some energy is always dissipated as heat due to friction, resistance, etc.
Efficiency = (useful energy output / total energy input) × 100% Efficiency = (useful power output / total power input) × 100% Energy "wasted" = total input − useful output
Worked Example 5An electric motor receives 400 J input and raises a 200 N load by 1.5 m. Useful output = 200×1.5 = 300 J. Efficiency = (300/400)×100 = 75%. Energy wasted = 100 J (as heat in motor).
Worked Example 6A light bulb has power input 100 W and radiates 10 W of light. Efficiency = (10/100)×100 = 10%. The other 90 W is wasted as heat.

Pressure in Solids and Liquids
Textbook §1.8 | Pressure

📘 Lesson Notes

PressureThe force acting per unit area on a surface. SI unit: pascal (Pa) = N/m². A large force concentrated on a small area gives high pressure; the same force spread over a large area gives low pressure.
P = F / A where P = pressure (Pa or N/m²), F = force (N), A = area (m²) Rearranged: F = P × A A = F / P 1 Pa = 1 N/m² 1 kPa = 1000 Pa
Worked Example 1 — Pressure on solidA brick of weight 50 N sits on a surface of area 0.02 m². P = 50/0.02 = 2500 Pa. If flipped to rest on its smaller face (0.005 m²): P = 50/0.005 = 10 000 Pa. Same force, different pressure!
Worked Example 2Atmospheric pressure is 100 000 Pa. Find the force on a window of area 0.5 m². F = P×A = 100 000 × 0.5 = 50 000 N = 50 kN.

Pressure in Liquids: P = ρgh

Pressure in a LiquidPressure in a liquid increases with depth. It depends on the density of the liquid and the depth below the free surface. Pressure acts equally in all directions at a given depth.
P = ρ × g × h where ρ = density of liquid (kg/m³), g = gravitational field strength (N/kg), h = depth below surface (m) P is in pascals (Pa)
Worked Example 3Find the pressure at a depth of 5 m in sea water (ρ = 1025 kg/m³, g = 10 N/kg).
P = 1025 × 10 × 5 = 51 250 Pa ≈ 51.3 kPa
Worked Example 4A dam holds water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³) to a depth of 20 m. What is the pressure at the base of the dam?
P = 1000 × 10 × 20 = 200 000 Pa = 200 kPa

Key Properties of Liquid Pressure

  • Pressure increases with depth (P ∝ h)
  • Pressure at the same depth is the same in all directions
  • Pressure depends on the density of the liquid (P ∝ ρ)
  • Pressure does NOT depend on the shape of the container (communicating vessels)
📷 Water flows from holes at different depths of a can — the deeper holes produce a more forceful jet, showing pressure increases with depth.
📷 U-tube manometer: liquid levels are equal when pressure is the same on both sides. If one side is connected to a gas supply, the pressure difference pushes the liquid down on the connected side.

Gas Pressure, Boyle's Law and Hydraulic Systems
Textbook §1.8 | Gas Pressure and Boyle's Law

📘 Lesson Notes

Atmospheric Pressure

The weight of the column of air above a point produces atmospheric pressure. At sea level, atmospheric pressure ≈ 101 000 Pa ≈ 100 kPa. It is measured with a barometer. Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude as there is less air above.

Gas Pressure

Gas pressure is caused by gas molecules bombarding the walls of their container. Reducing the volume increases the number of collisions per second per unit area, increasing the pressure.

Boyle's LawFor a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume. P ∝ 1/V or P × V = constant.
Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (at constant temperature, fixed mass) where P₁, P₂ = initial and final pressures, V₁, V₂ = initial and final volumes
Worked Example 1A gas has P₁ = 100 kPa and V₁ = 2.0 L. It is compressed to V₂ = 0.5 L at the same temperature. Find P₂.
P₂ = P₁V₁/V₂ = (100 × 2.0)/0.5 = 400 kPa. The pressure quadrupled as the volume was quartered.
Worked Example 2A 200 cm³ sample of gas at 150 kPa expands to 600 cm³. Find the new pressure.
P₂ = (150 × 200)/600 = 50 kPa

Hydraulic Systems

Liquids are almost incompressible. Pascal's Principle: a pressure applied to an enclosed liquid is transmitted equally throughout the liquid. This is the basis of hydraulic systems (brakes, lifts, car jacks).

Pressure transmitted equally: P = F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂ Force amplification: F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) If A₂ > A₁, then F₂ > F₁ (force multiplied)
Worked Example 3 — Hydraulic PressA small piston of area 0.002 m² has force 100 N applied. The large piston has area 0.02 m². Find the output force.
Pressure = 100/0.002 = 50 000 Pa (transmitted throughout liquid).
Output force = P × A₂ = 50 000 × 0.02 = 1000 N. Force is multiplied by 10!

Applications of hydraulic systems: Car brakes, forklift trucks, dentist's chair, aircraft controls, car jacks, construction equipment (excavators).

📘 Lesson Notes

All matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms, molecules, ions) in continuous motion. The kinetic particle model explains the properties of solids, liquids and gases in terms of the arrangement, separation and movement of these particles.

EvaporationWhen faster-moving molecules near the surface of a liquid gain enough energy to escape into the gaseous state. Occurs at temperatures below boiling point. Causes cooling of the remaining liquid.

Temperature and Kinetic Energy

Kinetic Particle Model — Key RelationshipThe temperature of a substance is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its particles. Higher temperature = particles move faster = greater average KE. This is true for all three states of matter.
Absolute ZeroThe lowest possible temperature (0 K = −273°C) at which particles have minimum kinetic energy (theoretically zero KE). Below absolute zero is impossible. The Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero.

Heating and Cooling Curves

When a substance is heated at a constant rate, a heating curve (temperature vs. time graph) shows characteristic flat sections at the melting and boiling points.

  • Rising sections: Temperature is increasing — energy is increasing the KE of particles (heating a solid, heating a liquid, heating a gas)
  • Flat sections (plateaux): Temperature stays constant during melting (at m.p.) and boiling (at b.p.) — energy is being used to break intermolecular bonds, NOT to raise temperature. This energy is called latent heat.
Melting Point (m.p.)The temperature at which a substance changes from solid to liquid (same temperature at which it freezes). Water: 0°C = 273 K.
Boiling Point (b.p.)The temperature at which a substance changes from liquid to gas throughout the bulk (not just at the surface). Water: 100°C = 373 K. Boiling point decreases at lower pressure (e.g., at high altitude).
Latent HeatThe energy absorbed or released during a change of state (melting or boiling) at constant temperature. "Latent" means hidden — the temperature doesn't change while this energy is exchanged.

Gas Laws: Boyle's Law and the Pressure–Temperature Law
Textbook §2.1.2 | Gas Laws; Pressure Law

📘 Lesson Notes

Kinetic Theory Explanation of Gas Pressure

Gas pressure results from gas molecules constantly colliding with the walls of the container. Each collision exerts a tiny force on the wall. The total of all these forces per unit area is the gas pressure. Higher temperature → particles move faster → harder, more frequent collisions → higher pressure.

Boyle's Law (Pressure–Volume) — Revision

Boyle's LawAt constant temperature, the pressure of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its volume: P ∝ 1/V. Equivalently: PV = constant.
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant temperature, fixed mass) Molecular explanation: reducing V → molecules collide with walls more frequently → pressure increases
Worked Example 1Gas in a sealed cylinder has P = 200 kPa and V = 3.0 L. Piston is pushed in until V = 1.2 L. Find new pressure.
P₂ = P₁V₁/V₂ = (200 × 3.0)/1.2 = 500 kPa

Pressure Law (Gay-Lussac's Law — Pressure–Temperature)

Pressure LawFor a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (in kelvin). P ∝ T (in K).
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (constant volume, fixed mass; T must be in KELVIN) Molecular explanation: higher T → molecules move faster → harder collisions with walls → pressure increases
Worked Example 2 — Pressure LawA sealed gas canister has P₁ = 100 kPa at T₁ = 27°C. It is heated to T₂ = 127°C. Find P₂.
Convert: T₁ = 27+273 = 300 K; T₂ = 127+273 = 400 K
P₂ = P₁ × T₂/T₁ = 100 × 400/300 = 133 kPa

Combined Gas Law

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ (fixed mass of gas, T in kelvin) Use when pressure, volume AND temperature all change
Worked Example 3 — Combined Gas LawA gas has P₁=100 kPa, V₁=4 L, T₁=300 K. It is compressed to V₂=2 L and heated to T₂=450 K. Find P₂.
P₂ = P₁V₁T₂/(T₁V₂) = (100×4×450)/(300×2) = 180 000/600 = 300 kPa
Worked Example 4 — Pressure Law in real contextA car tyre has pressure 200 kPa at 17°C (290 K). After driving, the tyre heats to 47°C (320 K). Find the new pressure.
P₂ = 200 × 320/290 = 220.7 kPa ≈ 221 kPa

📘 Lesson Notes

Thermal Expansion

When substances are heated, their particles vibrate more vigorously and push slightly further apart, causing the substance to expand. Most solids, liquids, and gases expand when heated.

Order of expansion (for same temperature rise): Gas > Liquid > Solid

Applications and Problems with Thermal Expansion

Application/ProblemExplanation
Expansion gaps in railway lines & bridgesAllow rails/beams to expand in summer without buckling
Bimetallic strip (thermostat)Two metals with different expansion rates; strip bends on heating — used in fire alarms, thermostats
ThermometersLiquid expands more than glass, visible in capillary tube
Riveting metal platesRed-hot rivets contract on cooling, clamping plates tightly
Overhead power lines (sag)Expand and sag in summer; strings are taut in winter
Fitting metal tyres on wheelsHeat tyre to expand, then place on wheel — shrinks to grip

The Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water behaves unusually: it contracts when cooled from 4°C to 0°C. Maximum density of water is at 4°C (1000 kg/m³). Below 4°C it expands and the density decreases. Ice (at 0°C) is less dense than water at 4°C — that's why ice floats!

Consequences: Aquatic life survives under ice in winter because water at 4°C sinks to the bottom, while less dense ice floats on top. Lakes freeze from the top down.

Thermometers and Temperature Scales

Thermometric PropertyA physical property that changes continuously and predictably with temperature, allowing temperature to be measured. Examples: length of liquid column, electrical resistance, EMF of a thermocouple, gas pressure.

Liquid-in-glass thermometers use the expansion of mercury or alcohol. Mercury: range −39°C to 357°C. Alcohol: range −115°C to 78°C. The glass capillary tube must be very narrow for the small expansion to be measurable.

Fixed points for Celsius scale: Lower fixed point = 0°C (ice point); Upper fixed point = 100°C (steam point at 101 kPa).

Worked Example — Bimetallic StripA strip is made of iron (low expansion rate) bonded to brass (high expansion rate). When heated, the brass expands more than the iron, so the strip curves with the brass on the outside of the curve. When cooled, brass contracts more than iron, so the strip curves the other way. This reversible bending is used in thermostats to make and break electrical contacts.

Conduction: Mechanism and Applications
Textbook §2.3.1 | Thermal Conduction

📘 Lesson Notes

ConductionThe flow of thermal energy (heat) through matter from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature, without the matter itself moving. The main method of heat transfer in solids.

Mechanism of Conduction

In metals (fast conduction): Metals have free (delocalised) electrons that can move through the metal. When one end is heated, the electrons gain kinetic energy and move faster. They collide with neighbouring atoms further away, transferring energy rapidly throughout the metal. This is why metals are excellent thermal conductors.

In non-metals (slow conduction): Non-metals have few or no free electrons. Heat transfer occurs only by atomic lattice vibrations — heated atoms vibrate more vigorously and cause neighbours to vibrate more. This is much slower than electron transfer.

In liquids and gases: Molecules are widely spaced and randomly arranged. No lattice vibrations possible; no free electrons. Therefore liquids and gases are very poor thermal conductors (excellent thermal insulators).

Good and Poor Conductors

Good Conductors (metals)Poor Conductors (insulators)
Copper (best), silver, aluminium, ironWood, glass, rubber, air, wool, plastic, cork
Fast electron transferLattice vibration only (or trapped air)
Used in: cooking pots, heatsinks, soldering ironsUsed in: handles, building insulation, clothing
Thermal InsulatorA material that conducts heat slowly. Good thermal insulators trap air (which is a poor conductor). Examples: wool, foam, down feathers, fiberglass, double-glazing.
Worked Example — Rate of ConductionConduction rate is greater when: (1) the material is a better conductor (e.g., copper vs iron), (2) the cross-sectional area is larger, (3) the rod is shorter, (4) the temperature difference is greater. A copper rod conducts about 5× faster than an iron rod of the same size under the same temperature difference.

Applications of Conduction

  • Cooking pots: Metal base conducts heat from stove to food; plastic handles insulate hands
  • Building insulation: Fiberglass, foam, double-glazing — all trap air to reduce conduction
  • Clothes: Wool, down, and fur trap air, reducing heat loss from body
  • Soldering irons: Metal tip conducts heat to solder; plastic handle insulates
  • Pipe insulation: Foam lagging reduces heat loss from hot water pipes

Convection: Mechanism, Currents and Applications
Textbook §2.3.2 | Convection

📘 Lesson Notes

ConvectionThe transfer of thermal energy through fluids (liquids and gases) by the movement of the fluid itself. Warm fluid becomes less dense and rises; cool fluid is denser and sinks. This sets up convection currents. Convection cannot occur in solids (particles cannot move freely).

Mechanism of Convection

  1. A region of fluid is heated → particles move faster → fluid expands → density decreases
  2. The warmer, less dense fluid experiences an upward buoyancy force and rises
  3. Cooler, denser fluid nearby is displaced and sinks to fill the space left below
  4. This forms a continuous convection current that circulates thermal energy throughout the fluid

Convection Current Examples

ExampleExplanation
Room heating (radiator)Hot air rises from radiator, cools at ceiling, sinks, returns to radiator — circular current heats room
Domestic hot water systemWater heated in boiler rises to header tank; cool water sinks back — natural convection circulates water
Sea breezes (daytime)Land heats faster than sea; warm air over land rises; cool sea air moves in → sea breeze
Land breezes (night)Land cools faster than sea; warm air over sea rises; cool land air moves toward sea → land breeze
Thermals (gliding)Warm air rises above hot ground; gliders ride these upward convection currents
Immersion heater positionHeater should be at BOTTOM of tank so hot water rises and convection heats entire tank
Why convection doesn't occur in solids:Solid particles vibrate about fixed positions — they cannot move from place to place, so bulk flow of matter is impossible. Conduction is the only mechanism in solids.
Worked Example — Convection in a roomAn electric heater is placed at the bottom of a room. The hot air near the heater expands, becomes less dense, and rises to the ceiling. It gradually cools, becomes denser, and sinks on the far side. This circular convection current eventually heats the whole room — much more effectively than a heater at the top.

Radiation: Emission, Absorption and the Greenhouse Effect
Textbook §2.3.3 | Thermal Radiation

📘 Lesson Notes

Thermal RadiationThe transfer of thermal energy by electromagnetic waves (mainly infrared radiation). Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does NOT require a material medium — it can travel through a vacuum. This is how heat from the Sun reaches Earth.

Properties of Thermal Radiation

  • Travels at the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s) in a vacuum
  • Is a form of electromagnetic radiation (mainly infrared)
  • All objects above 0 K (absolute zero) emit thermal radiation
  • The hotter the object, the greater the rate of emission
  • Can be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted by objects

Effect of Surface on Emission and Absorption

Surface TypeAbsorberEmitter
Dull, matt, blackBest absorber (absorbs most radiation)Best emitter (emits most radiation)
Shiny, polished, whitePoor absorber (reflects most radiation)Poor emitter (reflects radiation back)

Key rule: Good absorbers are good emitters; poor absorbers are poor emitters (reflectors).

Factors Affecting the Rate of Radiation Emission

  • Higher temperature → greater rate of emission
  • Larger surface area → greater rate of emission
  • Matt black surface → greater rate of emission
Applications
Black solar panels: Dull black surface absorbs maximum solar radiation.
White buildings in hot countries: Reflect radiation, staying cooler.
Black refrigerator cooling fins: Dull black surface radiates heat away efficiently.
Shiny tea/coffee flask: Polished surface reduces radiation emission, keeping drink hot.
Emergency blankets (silver foil): Reflect radiation back to body, reducing heat loss.

The Greenhouse Effect

The Earth's atmosphere acts like glass in a greenhouse. Short-wavelength visible light and UV from the Sun pass through the atmosphere easily. The Earth's surface absorbs this and re-emits longer-wavelength infrared radiation. Carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and water vapour absorb this infrared and re-radiate it back to Earth, trapping heat.

Increased CO₂ from burning fossil fuels is enhancing this effect, causing global warming. Consequences: rising sea levels, extreme weather, habitat loss.
