General Properties of Waves

§3.1

📖 Lesson Notes

Progressive Wave

A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. A leaf on a pond bobs up and down as a ripple passes — it does not travel with the wave.

Transverse Wave

Vibrations are perpendicular to the direction of wave travel. Examples: all electromagnetic waves (light, radio, X-rays), water waves, waves on a rope. Crests and troughs are visible in a displacement–distance graph.

Longitudinal Wave

Vibrations are parallel to the direction of wave travel. Examples: sound, seismic P-waves. Consists of alternating compressions (particles closer together) and rarefactions (particles further apart).

Key Wave Terms
  • Amplitude (a) — max displacement from equilibrium; unit: m. Related to energy carried.
  • Wavelength (λ) — distance between two successive crests (or compressions); unit: m.
  • Frequency (f) — complete waves passing a point per second; unit: Hz.
  • Wave speed (v) — distance a wavefront travels per second; unit: m/s.
  • Period (T) — time for one complete wave; T = 1/f; unit: s.
  • Phase — two points are in phase if they have the same displacement and move in the same direction simultaneously (e.g. exactly one wavelength apart).
Core Wave Formulae
v = f λ
T = 1 / f   |   f = 1 / T
v = λ / T
v = wave speed (m/s) · f = frequency (Hz) · λ = wavelength (m) · T = period (s)
Worked Example 1 — Finding wavelength

A wave travels at 4 cm/s with frequency 2 Hz. Find its wavelength.

λ = v/f = 4 / 2 = 2 cm
Worked Example 2 — Finding speed

Sound has frequency 440 Hz and wavelength 0.75 m. Find its speed.

v = f × λ = 440 × 0.75 = 330 m/s
Worked Example 3 — Period

A source produces 50 waves per second. Find the period.

T = 1/f = 1/50 = 0.02 s
Worked Example 4 — Reading a wave graph

A displacement–distance graph shows crests 0.30 m apart; 10 complete waves pass a point in 5 s. Find wave speed.

λ = 0.30 m; f = 10/5 = 2 Hz
v = fλ = 2 × 0.30 = 0.60 m/s
📐 A displacement–distance graph shows wavelength λ between successive crests and amplitude a from the equilibrium line to the crest. Points exactly one wavelength apart are in phase; points half a wavelength apart are exactly out of phase (antiphase).

Ripple Tank Experiments — Water waves in a ripple tank demonstrate three key behaviours:

  • Reflection: waves bounce from barriers; angle of incidence = angle of reflection (measured from normal to barrier).
  • Refraction: waves pass from deep to shallow water; speed decreases, wavelength decreases, direction changes (unless hitting boundary at 90°). Frequency stays the same.
  • Diffraction: waves spread as they pass through a gap or around an obstacle. Spreading is greatest when the gap width ≈ wavelength. Wider gap → less spreading; narrower gap → more semicircular spreading.

  3. 3Count crests passing a fixed point in 10 s to find frequency. Calculate v = fλ.
  4. 4Place a flat barrier at 30° to the wave fronts. Observe and sketch the reflected pattern. Measure i and r — confirm i = r.
  5. 5Use a sloped ramp to create a shallow region. Observe the wave slowing, λ decreasing, and direction changing.
  6. 6Insert a barrier with two different gap sizes. Compare diffraction patterns. Sketch both.

Reflection of Light

§3.2.1

📖 Lesson Notes

Law of Reflection

The angle of incidence (i) equals the angle of reflection (r). Both angles are measured from the normal — the perpendicular to the mirror at the point of incidence. The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal are coplanar (lie in the same plane).

Regular vs Diffuse Reflection
  • Regular (specular): parallel rays remain parallel after reflection — occurs at very smooth, polished surfaces (mirrors, still water).
  • Diffuse: parallel rays scatter in all directions after reflection — occurs at rough surfaces (paper, walls). This is how most non-luminous objects are visible.
Image in a Plane Mirror — Five Characteristics
  • Virtual — cannot be projected on a screen; rays appear to diverge from behind the mirror.
  • Same size as the object (unit magnification).
  • Same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front.
  • Laterally inverted — left and right are swapped.
  • Upright (not inverted vertically).
Reflection
Angle of incidence (i) = Angle of reflection (r)
Image distance = Object distance (from mirror)
Angles measured from the normal, not from the mirror surface
Worked Example 1 — Angles

A ray strikes a mirror at 40° to the mirror surface. Find angle of incidence and angle of reflection.

Angle to mirror = 40°; angle to normal = 90° − 40° = 50°
i = r = 50°
Worked Example 2 — Image position

An object is 8 cm in front of a plane mirror. Describe the image.

Image is 8 cm behind mirror; same size; virtual; upright; laterally inverted.
Worked Example 3 — Periscope

Two plane mirrors, each at 45° to the incoming ray, form a periscope. Trace the ray path.

First mirror: ray at 45° → reflected through 90°.
Second mirror: ray at 45° → reflected through 90°. Total: 180° deviation.
Worked Example 4 — Pinhole camera

A tree 10 m tall stands 20 m from a 0.15 m long pinhole camera. Find the image height.

Image height / Object height = camera length / object distance
Image height = (0.15 / 20) × 10 = 0.075 m = 7.5 cm

The pinhole camera shows light travels in straight lines. A small hole allows rays from an object to form a real, inverted image on the screen. Making the pinhole larger gives a brighter but blurrier image. The pinhole camera creates real images; a mirror creates virtual images.

  4. 4Remove mirror; measure the angle of reflection from the normal.
  5. 5Repeat for i = 40°, 50°, 60°. Record all results in a table. Conclude: i = r in all cases.
  6. 6Use two mirrors at 45° to build a simple periscope. Look over a barrier to verify it works.

Refraction and Snell's Law

§3.2.2

📖 Lesson Notes

Refraction

The bending of a wave as it passes from one medium into another due to a change in speed. Light slows down entering a denser medium (e.g. glass) and bends toward the normal. It speeds up leaving a denser medium and bends away from the normal. A ray hitting the boundary along the normal (90°) is not deflected but still changes speed.

Refractive Index (n)

A dimensionless measure of how much a medium reduces light speed compared with a vacuum. For a ray going from air into a material: n = sin i / sin r, where i is the angle in air (less dense) and r is the angle in the material (denser).

Snell's Law and Refractive Index
n = sin i / sin r
n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
n = c / v   (speed in vacuum ÷ speed in medium)
Real depth / Apparent depth = n
n = refractive index (no units) · c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s · v = speed of light in medium
Worked Example 1 — Finding n

A ray hits glass at i = 45°; refracted angle r = 28°. Find n.

n = sin 45° / sin 28° = 0.707 / 0.469 = 1.51
Worked Example 2 — Finding refracted angle

Glass has n = 1.5; ray strikes at i = 30°. Find r.

sin r = sin 30° / 1.5 = 0.500 / 1.5 = 0.333
r = sin⁻¹(0.333) = 19.5°
Worked Example 3 — Speed in medium

Water has n = 1.33. Find the speed of light in water.

v = c/n = (3 × 10⁸) / 1.33 = 2.26 × 10⁸ m/s
Worked Example 4 — Apparent depth

A fish appears 60 cm deep in water (n = 1.33). Find the real depth.

Real depth = n × apparent depth = 1.33 × 0.60 = 0.80 m

Dispersion: When white light enters a glass prism, different colours refract by different amounts because they travel at slightly different speeds in glass (violet slowest → most bent; red fastest → least bent). This separates white light into its spectrum: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet.

Total Internal Reflection

§3.2.3

📖 Lesson Notes

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

When light travels from a denser to a less dense medium and the angle of incidence in the denser medium exceeds the critical angle, all light is completely reflected back — no refraction occurs. Two conditions required: (1) light must be in the denser medium, (2) the angle of incidence must exceed the critical angle.

Critical Angle (c)

The angle of incidence in the denser medium at which the refracted ray just travels along the boundary (angle of refraction = 90°). For angles below c, partial refraction and partial reflection occur. At exactly c, refracted ray grazes the boundary. Above c, total internal reflection.

Critical Angle
sin c = 1 / n
c = sin⁻¹ (1/n)
c = critical angle · n = refractive index of denser medium relative to air
Worked Example 1 — Critical angle of glass

Glass has n = 1.50. Find its critical angle.

sin c = 1/1.50 = 0.667; c = sin⁻¹(0.667) = 41.8° ≈ 42°
Worked Example 2 — Diamond sparkle

Diamond has n = 2.42. Find its critical angle and explain why diamonds sparkle.

sin c = 1/2.42 = 0.413; c = 24.4°
Very small critical angle → light undergoes many TIR reflections inside the diamond before escaping → brilliant sparkle.
Worked Example 3 — Optical fibre check

Glass fibre has n = 1.6; light ray makes 50° angle with the fibre axis. Does TIR occur?

sin c = 1/1.6 = 0.625; c = 38.7°
Angle to the boundary = 90° − 50° = 40° > 38.7° → TIR occurs ✓
Worked Example 4 — Finding n from critical angle

The critical angle of water is 48.8°. Find n.

n = 1/sin 48.8° = 1/0.752 = 1.33

Applications of TIR:

  • Optical fibres: Light bounces along glass or plastic fibres by repeated TIR. Used in endoscopes (medical imaging inside the body) and broadband fibre-optic telecommunications. Very little energy is lost over long distances.
  • Prisms in binoculars/periscopes: 45°–45°–90° glass prisms reflect light by TIR (at 45° > c ≈ 42°). More efficient than silvered mirrors — no energy lost in the silver coating.
  • Road cat's-eyes: retroreflectors use prismatic TIR to return car headlight beams to drivers.
  • Diamond cutting: many flat facets maximise TIR and brilliance.

🔬 Classroom Activity — Finding Critical Angle

Materials: semicircular glass block, ray box, protractor, white paper

  1. 1Place the semicircular block flat-side down. Direct a ray through the curved face toward the centre of the flat face (no refraction at curved surface).
  2. 2Slowly increase the angle of incidence at the flat face. Observe the refracted ray in air bending further away from the normal.
  3. 3Continue until the refracted ray just disappears — this is the critical angle. Measure and record.
  4. 4Calculate n = 1/sin c. Compare with n = 1.5 for standard glass.
  5. 5Increase the angle past c — observe total internal reflection. The reflected ray obeys i = r.
  6. 6Draw diagrams for all three cases: i < c, i = c, i > c.

Lenses — Converging and Diverging

§3.2.4

📖 Lesson Notes

Converging (Convex) Lens

Thicker at centre; refracts parallel rays to converge at the principal focus F on the far side. Focal length f = distance from optical centre O to F. Can form real or virtual images depending on object position.

Diverging (Concave) Lens

Thinner at centre; refracts parallel rays so they appear to diverge from a virtual focus on the same side as the incoming light. Always produces a virtual, upright, diminished image for real objects.

Three Ray Rules for Converging Lens
  • Ray parallel to axis → refracts through far focal point F.
  • Ray through optical centre O → passes straight through undeviated.
  • Ray through near focal point F → exits parallel to axis.
Lens Equations
1/f = 1/u + 1/v
Magnification m = v / u = image height / object height
Power P = 1/f (f in metres); unit: dioptre (D)
u = object distance · v = image distance · f = focal length (all in same units)
Worked Example 1 — Object beyond 2F

Object 30 cm from converging lens, f = 10 cm. Find image position and type.

1/v = 1/10 − 1/30 = 3/30 − 1/30 = 2/30; v = 15 cm
m = 15/30 = 0.5 → real, inverted, diminished
Worked Example 2 — Object between F and 2F

Object 15 cm from converging lens, f = 10 cm.

1/v = 1/10 − 1/15 = 3/30 − 2/30 = 1/30; v = 30 cm
m = 30/15 = 2 → real, inverted, magnified
Worked Example 3 — Magnifying glass (inside F)

Object 6 cm from converging lens, f = 10 cm.

1/v = 1/10 − 1/6 = 3/30 − 5/30 = −2/30; v = −15 cm
Negative v → virtual image on same side as object; m = 15/6 = 2.5 → virtual, upright, magnified
Worked Example 4 — Lens power

A lens has f = +0.25 m. State its type and power.

P = 1/f = 1/0.25 = +4 D (converging)

The Eye and Camera

§3.2.5

📖 Lesson Notes

The Human Eye

Forms a real, inverted image on the retina. The cornea does ~70% of refraction (fixed). The flexible crystalline lens fine-tunes focus by changing shape (accommodation).

  • Ciliary muscles relaxed → lens thin (long f) → focuses distant objects.
  • Ciliary muscles contracted → lens fat (short f) → focuses near objects.
  • Near point: ~25 cm (closest clear vision). Far point: infinity (normal eye).
Defects and Corrections
  • Myopia (short sight): eyeball too long or lens too strong → image in front of retina → cannot see far. Corrected with diverging lens.
  • Hyperopia (long sight): eyeball too short or lens too weak → image behind retina → cannot focus near. Corrected with converging lens.
Worked Example 1 — Correcting short sight

Far point = 2.0 m. Find the power of the correcting lens (to see infinity).

Diverging lens must form virtual image of ∞ at −2.0 m (far point).
1/f = 1/v − 1/u = 1/(−2.0) − 0 = −0.5; P = −0.5 D
Worked Example 2 — Correcting long sight

Near point = 100 cm. Find power of lens for normal reading at 25 cm.

Lens forms virtual image at −100 cm for object at 25 cm.
1/f = 1/25 + 1/(−100) = 4/100 − 1/100 = 3/100; f = 33.3 cm = 0.333 m
P = 1/0.333 = +3.0 D
Worked Example 3 — Camera image size

Tree 8 m tall, 40 m from camera; lens-film distance = 5 cm. Find image height.

m = v/u = 0.05/40 = 0.00125
Image height = 0.00125 × 8 = 0.01 m = 1 cm
Worked Example 4 — Eye at near point

Lens-retina distance = 2.0 cm; object at 25 cm. Find focal length needed.

1/f = 1/25 + 1/2 = 0.04 + 0.50 = 0.54 cm⁻¹
f = 1/0.54 = 1.85 cm

A camera focuses by moving the lens toward or away from the film/sensor. The eye focuses by changing the shape of the lens (accommodation). In both, the image is real, inverted, and diminished (object is beyond 2F).

Materials: converging lens, screen at fixed 2 cm behind lens (retina model), illuminated object, optical bench

  2. 2Move object closer — image blurs on fixed screen (accommodation limit).
  3. 3Simulate short sight: move screen slightly further back. Image forms in front of screen. Introduce a diverging lens — image sharpens on screen.
  4. 4Simulate long sight: move screen slightly forward. Image forms behind screen. Introduce a converging lens — image sharpens.
  5. 5Draw annotated diagrams for: normal vision, short sight + correction, long sight + correction.
  6. 6Discuss why the correction lens does not focus the image itself — it merely shifts the object distance so the eye lens can do so.

  1. Name the two structures in the eye responsible for refraction. Which does most of the refraction and which is adjustable?
  2. Explain the process of accommodation. What happens to the ciliary muscles and the lens shape when the eye focuses from far to near?
  3. A short-sighted person has a far point of 1.5 m. Find the power of the correcting lens. State the type of lens required.

  7. Describe three ways a camera controls the amount of light reaching the film, and explain the physics of each.

The Electromagnetic Spectrum

§3.3

📖 Lesson Notes

Properties Common to All EM Waves
  • All travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum (same speed in air to 3 s.f.).
  • All are transverse waves (oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to direction of travel).
  • All obey v = fλ (with v = c in vacuum).
  • All can be reflected, refracted, and diffracted.
  • All carry energy; higher frequency → greater energy; lower frequency → less energy.
  • All travel through vacuum (unlike sound).

Light has wavelength 5 × 10⁻⁷ m. Find frequency.

Worked Example 3 — Identifying region

Radiation has λ = 3 × 10⁻¹⁰ m. Identify the EM region.

3 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.3 nm → falls in X-ray region (0.01–10 nm)
Worked Example 4 — Digital vs analogue

State two advantages of digital signals over analogue signals in communications.

(a) More resistant to noise/interference — signal can be regenerated perfectly (just 0 or 1).
(b) Can carry more information per second (higher data rate).

Sound Waves

§3.4

📖 Lesson Notes

Sound — Nature and Production

Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal, progressive wave. It is produced by vibrating objects (speaker cones, vocal cords, guitar strings). Air particles vibrate back and forth along the direction of travel, creating alternating compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure). Sound cannot travel through a vacuum — a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) is required. The bell-in-jar experiment demonstrates this: as air is pumped out, the bell becomes inaudible while still visible.

Speed of Sound
  • In air at room temperature: ~340 m/s.
  • Speed is greater in denser/less compressible materials: solids > liquids > gases.
  • Speed in air increases with temperature.

Limits of Audibility

Normal human hearing: 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz (20 kHz). The upper limit decreases with age. Below 20 Hz = infrasound. Above 20 kHz = ultrasound.

Sound Wave Equations
v = f λ   (wave equation)
v = 2d / t   (echo method)
d = distance to reflecting surface · t = total time for echo to return
Worked Example 1 — Echo distance

A clap echoes off a cliff in 0.5 s. Find the distance to the cliff.

d = v × t/2 = 340 × 0.5/2 = 85 m
Worked Example 2 — Sound frequency

Sound has v = 340 m/s and λ = 0.85 m. Find frequency.

f = v/λ = 340/0.85 = 400 Hz
Worked Example 3 — Sonar depth sounding

Sonar pulse returns in 0.8 s. Speed in water = 1400 m/s. Find ocean depth.

d = 1400 × 0.8/2 = 560 m
Worked Example 4 — Wavelength in different media

440 Hz sound: find λ in (a) air (340 m/s) and (b) water (1400 m/s).

(a) λ = 340/440 = 0.77 m
(b) λ = 1400/440 = 3.18 m (f unchanged; λ increases with speed)

Echoes and reverberation: Echoes are reflected sounds. Reverberation occurs when the reflecting surface is <15 m away — the echo merges with the original sound, prolonging it. Concert hall acoustics are designed to optimise a small amount of reverberation for a full sound without confusion. Soft furnishings and angled walls absorb and scatter sound.

Pitch, Loudness and Ultrasound

§3.4

📖 Lesson Notes

Pitch and Frequency

Pitch — the perceived highness or lowness of a sound — depends on frequency. Higher frequency = higher pitch. A violin string pressed down shortens → higher f → higher pitch. A longer organ pipe resonates at lower f → lower pitch.

Loudness and Amplitude

Loudness depends on the amplitude of the wave — the magnitude of the pressure variation. Larger amplitude = louder sound = more energy per second. An oscilloscope displays waveforms: the height of the trace = amplitude (loudness); the spacing of peaks = period (frequency/pitch).

Ultrasound

Sound with frequency >20 000 Hz (20 kHz). Produced by piezoelectric transducers (crystals that vibrate when an alternating voltage is applied). Uses pulse-echo technique: a pulse is emitted → reflects at boundaries between different densities → time of echo measured → depth = v × t/2.

Ultrasound and Oscilloscope
d = v × t / 2   (depth from pulse-echo)
f = 1 / T   (from oscilloscope trace: T = period)
Worked Example 1 — Reading an oscilloscope

A CRO trace shows one cycle occupies 4 divisions (timebase = 2.5 ms/div). Find f.

T = 4 × 2.5 ms = 10 ms = 0.010 s
f = 1/T = 1/0.010 = 100 Hz
Worked Example 2 — Medical ultrasound depth

Ultrasound (v = 1500 m/s) echo returns in 130 μs. Find depth.

d = 1500 × (130 × 10⁻⁶) / 2 = 1500 × 65 × 10⁻⁶ = 0.0975 m ≈ 9.8 cm
Worked Example 3 — Flaw detection in steel

Ultrasound (v = 6000 m/s in steel) detects a crack; echo returns in 8 μs. Find depth.

d = 6000 × (8 × 10⁻⁶) / 2 = 0.024 m = 2.4 cm
Worked Example 4 — Comparing CRO traces

Trace A: large amplitude, longer period. Trace B: small amplitude, shorter period. Describe sounds.

Trace A: loud, low pitch (large amplitude, long period → low f)
Trace B: quiet, high pitch (small amplitude, short period → high f)

Uses of Ultrasound:

  • Pre-natal scanning: safe (non-ionising), produces real-time images of foetus. No radiation risk to mother or baby.
  • Industrial NDT (Non-Destructive Testing): detects cracks and voids in metal components, welds, and aircraft parts without cutting them open.
  • SONAR: depth sounding, fish finding, submarine detection.
  • Ultrasonic cleaning: high-frequency vibration removes dirt from jewellery and dental instruments.
  • Kidney stone removal: focused ultrasound shatters stones without surgery (lithotripsy).
  • Measurement of blood flow (Doppler ultrasound): frequency shifts show blood flow speed and direction.

Static Electricity

§4.2.1

📖 Lesson Notes

Electric Charge and Friction

Rubbing two insulators together transfers electrons from one to the other. Material gaining electrons → negatively charged. Material losing electrons → positively charged. The charge remains in place (static) because insulators prevent electron flow. Example: polythene rubbed with wool → polythene becomes negative (gains e⁻); acetate rubbed with cloth → acetate becomes positive (loses e⁻).

Law of Electrostatics

Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. Force decreases as separation increases. Charge is measured in coulombs (C). Charge on one electron = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

Conductors and Insulators
  • Conductors (metals, carbon): free electrons move throughout the material. Charge flows away instantly when a conductor is earthed.
  • Insulators (polythene, glass, rubber): electrons tightly bound — charge stays where it was deposited.
  • Earthing: connecting to ground neutralises charge by allowing electrons to flow away or in.
Electric Fields

Region around a charged object where a force acts on other charges. Direction = direction of force on a positive test charge. Field lines go from positive to negative. Closer field lines = stronger field. Between two parallel plates with opposite charges: uniform field (parallel equidistant lines).

Worked Example 1 — Number of electrons transferred

A polythene rod has charge −4.8 × 10⁻⁹ C. How many electrons were transferred to it?

n = Q/e = (4.8 × 10⁻⁹) / (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 3.0 × 10¹⁰ electrons
Worked Example 2 — Electrostatic induction

A positively charged rod is brought near the cap of an uncharged gold-leaf electroscope. Explain what happens.

Free electrons in the cap are attracted toward the rod → move up to the cap.
The leaf and plate lose electrons → become positively charged → leaf diverges (like charges repel).
When rod is removed, electrons redistribute → leaf falls back.
Worked Example 3 — Laser printer (static electricity)

Describe how static electricity enables a laser printer to work.

A drum is given a positive charge pattern (image to print).
Negatively charged toner is attracted to the positive regions on the drum.
The toner is transferred to paper, then fused by heat.
Worked Example 4 — Electrostatic paint spraying

Explain why electrostatically charged paint droplets give a more even coat.

Paint droplets are charged; the metal object is earthed (oppositely charged).
Unlike charges attract → droplets attracted to all surfaces, including hidden undersides.
Like-charged droplets repel each other → spread evenly, no clumping.

Hazards of static electricity: Sparks from static can ignite flammable vapours (petrol refuelling → earth bonding wire used). Aircraft can build up charge in flight → earthed by a conducting discharge wire before personnel touch it. Lightning rods on buildings conduct charge safely to earth. Electronic components (chips) can be destroyed by static discharge — handled with earthed wrist straps.

Electric Current and Charge

§4.2.2

📖 Lesson Notes

Electric Current

The flow of electric charge through a conductor. In metals, current is carried by free electrons moving from the negative terminal to the positive terminal (opposite to conventional current). Conventional current direction: from positive to negative terminal. Measured in amperes (A) using an ammeter in series.

Charge, Current and Time

Current = rate of flow of charge. If Q coulombs pass in t seconds: I = Q/t. Rearranging: Q = I × t.

Potential Difference (p.d.) and EMF
  • p.d. (voltage V): energy transferred per unit charge between two points. V = W/Q where W = work done (J), Q = charge (C). Measured with a voltmeter in parallel.
  • EMF (electromotive force): energy given per unit charge by the source (cell, generator). Also in volts.
Current and Charge
Q = I × t
I = Q / t
V = W / Q
Q = charge (C) · I = current (A) · t = time (s) · V = p.d. (V) · W = energy (J)
Worked Example 1 — Charge from current

Current 2.5 A flows for 4 minutes. Find total charge.

t = 4 × 60 = 240 s
Q = 2.5 × 240 = 600 C
Worked Example 2 — Current from charge

3600 C of charge passes in 10 minutes. Find current.

I = Q/t = 3600/600 = 6.0 A
Worked Example 3 — Energy from voltage

A 12 V battery moves 5 C around a circuit. How much energy is transferred?

W = V × Q = 12 × 5 = 60 J
Worked Example 4 — Number of electrons

A current of 0.5 A flows for 1 minute. How many electrons pass? (e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)

Q = 0.5 × 60 = 30 C
n = 30 / (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 1.875 × 10²⁰ electrons

An ammeter must be in series with a component (it has very low resistance). A voltmeter must be in parallel across a component (it has very high resistance so negligible current flows through it). Direction of current: conventional current flows from + to −; electrons flow from − to + in the external circuit.

Voltage, Resistance and Ohm's Law

§4.2.3

📖 Lesson Notes

Resistance

Opposition to current flow. Arises because electrons collide with positive ions in the metal lattice. Unit: ohm (Ω). Increases with: longer wire, smaller cross-sectional area, higher temperature (for metals), higher resistivity material.

Ohm's Law

For a metallic conductor at constant temperature, current is directly proportional to the potential difference. R = V/I = constant. I–V graph: straight line through origin. Components obeying this are ohmic.

Non-Ohmic Components
  • Filament lamp: R increases as temperature rises (S-shaped I–V curve). Non-ohmic.
  • Diode: conducts in one direction only; very high R in reverse bias.
  • Thermistor (NTC): R decreases as temperature increases. Used in temperature sensing circuits.
  • LDR: R decreases as light intensity increases. Used in light-sensing circuits.
Ohm's Law
V = I × R
I = V / R
R = V / I
V = p.d. (V) · I = current (A) · R = resistance (Ω)
Worked Example 1 — Finding R

6 V battery drives 0.3 A through a resistor. Find R.

R = V/I = 6/0.3 = 20 Ω
Worked Example 2 — Finding current

240 V appliance with R = 960 Ω. Find current.

I = V/R = 240/960 = 0.25 A
Worked Example 3 — Finding voltage

50 Ω resistor carries 0.8 A. Find voltage across it.

V = IR = 0.8 × 50 = 40 V
Worked Example 4 — Series resistors

Resistors 4 Ω, 6 Ω, 10 Ω in series with 20 V supply. Find current and voltage across each.

R_total = 4 + 6 + 10 = 20 Ω; I = 20/20 = 1 A
V₁ = 4 V; V₂ = 6 V; V₃ = 10 V (total = 20 V ✓)

Electrical Power and Energy

§4.2.4

📖 Lesson Notes

Electrical Power

Rate at which electrical energy is transferred. P = IV. Unit: watt (W). Also P = I²R (heating effect) and P = V²/R.

Electrical Energy

E = Pt = IVt. Unit: joule (J). Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh). 1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J. Cost = energy (kWh) × price per kWh.

Power and Energy
P = I × V | P = I² × R | P = V² / R
E = P × t | E = I × V × t
P = power (W) · I = current (A) · V = voltage (V) · R = resistance (Ω) · E = energy (J) · t = time (s)
Worked Example 1 — Kettle power

A kettle draws 8.7 A from 230 V. Find its power.

P = IV = 8.7 × 230 = 2001 W ≈ 2 kW
Worked Example 2 — Energy and cost

The 2 kW kettle is used for 10 minutes. Find energy in J and cost at $0.15/kWh.

E = Pt = 2000 × 600 = 1.2 × 10⁶ J
kWh = 2 × 10/60 = 0.333 kWh; Cost = 0.333 × 0.15 = $0.050
Worked Example 3 — Heating effect

A 10 Ω resistor carries 2 A. Find power dissipated.

P = I²R = 4 × 10 = 40 W
Worked Example 4 — Monthly bill

60 W bulb on 8 h/day for 30 days at $0.12/kWh. Find energy and cost.

Energy = 0.060 × 8 × 30 = 14.4 kWh
Cost = 14.4 × 0.12 = $1.73

The heating effect of current (I²R heating): electric fires, kettles, toasters, incandescent bulbs all convert electrical energy to thermal energy. Fuses rely on this — a thin wire melts when current exceeds the rated value, breaking the circuit. The rate of heat production is proportional to I²R, so a thinner wire (higher R) heats faster for the same current.

Series and Parallel Circuits

§4.3.2

📖 Lesson Notes

Series Circuits
  • Single path for current — current is the same everywhere: I₁ = I₂ = I.
  • Total voltage = sum of component voltages: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law).
  • Total resistance = sum of individual resistances: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃.
  • If one component fails (open circuit) → all fail.
Parallel Circuits
  • Multiple current paths — voltage across each branch is the same: V₁ = V₂ = V.
  • Total current = sum of branch currents: I = I₁ + I₂ (Kirchhoff's Current Law).
  • Effective resistance is less than any individual branch: 1/R_eff = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂.
  • Components can be switched independently — used in domestic wiring.
Series and Parallel Resistors
Series: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Parallel: 1/R_eff = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
Series: V = V₁ + V₂ (KVL)  |  Parallel: I = I₁ + I₂ (KCL)
Worked Example 1 — Series circuit

Resistors 6 Ω and 12 Ω in series with 18 V supply. Find R_total, I, and V across each.

R = 6 + 12 = 18 Ω; I = 18/18 = 1 A
V₁ = 1×6 = 6 V; V₂ = 1×12 = 12 V (6+12 = 18 V ✓)
Worked Example 2 — Parallel circuit

Resistors 6 Ω and 12 Ω in parallel with 12 V. Find R_eff and current in each branch.

1/R = 1/6 + 1/12 = 3/12; R_eff = 4 Ω
I₁ = 12/6 = 2 A; I₂ = 12/12 = 1 A; I_total = 3 A (= 12/4 ✓)
Worked Example 3 — Mixed circuit

3 Ω and 6 Ω in parallel; this in series with 4 Ω and 12 V supply. Find total current.

1/R_p = 1/3 + 1/6 = 3/6; R_p = 2 Ω
R_total = 2 + 4 = 6 Ω; I = 12/6 = 2 A
Worked Example 4 — Cells in combination

Two 1.5 V cells: (a) series, (b) parallel. State e.m.f. and advantage of each.

(a) Series: e.m.f. = 3.0 V — doubled voltage for larger current.
(b) Parallel: e.m.f. = 1.5 V — same voltage but lasts twice as long (greater capacity).

Domestic wiring uses parallel: each appliance receives the full mains voltage (230 V), can be switched independently, and a fault in one does not affect others. The total current from the supply = sum of all branch currents, so many appliances together demand a large total current.

Hazards of Mains Electricity
  • Damaged insulation: exposes live wire — touching causes current through body.
  • Overheated cables: excessive current → wires overheat → fire risk.
  • Damp conditions: water reduces skin resistance (dry ~10 000 Ω; wet ~1 000 Ω) → much greater shock current. Potentially fatal.
  • Overloaded sockets: too many appliances → excessive total current → overheating.
Three-Pin Plug Wiring (UK)
  • Live (L) — Brown: alternating current at 230 V; dangerous.
  • Neutral (N) — Blue: return at ~0 V; safe when circuit is switched off.
  • Earth (E) — Green/Yellow: safety wire at 0 V; connected to metal casing. If live wire touches casing → large current flows → fuse blows → circuit breaks → casing safe.
Fuses and Safety Devices
  • Fuse: thin wire melts if current exceeds rated value → breaks circuit. Always in the live wire. Choose lowest rating above normal operating current.
  • Circuit breaker / RCD: electromagnetic switch — trips and can be reset. Reacts faster than fuses to earth faults.
  • Double insulation: plastic casing + all-plastic internal components → no path for current to reach user → no earth wire needed (e.g. hairdryer, power drill).

Live → drum → earth wire → ground: large current flows instantaneously.
Large current → fuse blows → circuit breaks → drum now at 0 V → user safe.

Why does a plastic-cased hairdryer not need an earth wire?

Outer casing is plastic (insulator) → even if live wire touches casing, no current flows to user.

Magnetism and Magnetic Fields

§4.1

📖 Lesson Notes

Properties of Magnets
  • Every magnet has two poles: North (N) and South (S). Poles always occur in pairs.
  • Law of magnetic poles: Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
  • Force decreases with increasing separation.
  • Magnetic materials (ferromagnets): iron, steel, nickel, cobalt.
  • Temporary magnets (soft iron): easily magnetised/demagnetised. Used in relay cores, transformer cores.
  • Permanent magnets (hard steel, alnico, neodymium): retain magnetism. Used in motors, speakers, compasses.
Magnetic Fields and Field Lines
  • Direction: from N to S outside the magnet; from S to N inside.
  • Closer field lines = stronger field.
  • Lines never cross.
  • Between opposite poles of two flat magnets: uniform field (parallel lines).
  • Neutral point: where two fields cancel; no net force.
Induced Magnetism

Unmagnetised soft iron brought near a magnet becomes temporarily magnetised — the pole nearest the permanent magnet's N becomes S (unlike poles attract). Remove the magnet → soft iron loses its magnetism. Steel retains magnetism → becomes permanently magnetised.

Worked Example 1 — Plotting field with compass

Describe how to plot the magnetic field of a bar magnet using a plotting compass.

Place magnet on paper. Position compass near N pole. Mark position of N end of compass needle with a dot.
Move compass so S end is at previous dot. Mark new N end. Repeat until a complete field line is traced.
Arrow direction: N pole of magnet → outward along field line.
Worked Example 2 — Temporary vs permanent

A chain of iron nails hangs from a bar magnet. The chain collapses when the magnet is removed. Explain.

Iron nails have magnetism induced in them (soft iron → temporary).
When the bar magnet is removed, induced magnetism disappears → nails lose attraction → chain collapses.
Worked Example 3 — Demagnetising methods

State three ways to demagnetise a permanent magnet.

(a) Heating above Curie temperature → thermal energy disrupts domain alignment.
(b) Hammering/dropping → mechanical shock randomises domains.
(c) AC demagnetiser → decreasing alternating field leaves domains randomly oriented.
Worked Example 4 — Uses: electromagnet vs permanent

Why is an electromagnet, rather than a permanent magnet, used in a scrap metal crane?

An electromagnet can be switched off to release the load.
A permanent magnet cannot be easily switched → cannot release the metal once attached.

Electromagnetism

§4.5

📖 Lesson Notes

Magnetic Field Around a Current-Carrying Wire
  • A straight wire carrying current is surrounded by circular magnetic field lines.
  • Right-hand grip rule (straight wire): thumb points in direction of conventional current; fingers curl in direction of field.
  • The field is stronger: closer to the wire; with larger current.
  • A solenoid (coil) creates a uniform field inside like a bar magnet. The ends are N and S poles.
  • Right-hand grip rule (solenoid): curl fingers in direction of current in coils; thumb points to N pole.
Motor Effect — Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field experiences a force (the motor effect). Force is maximum when current is perpendicular to field. Zero when current is parallel to field.

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Hold thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the left hand mutually at right angles. Index = direction of magnetic Field; Middle = direction of conventional Current; Thumb = direction of Motion (force on wire).

Electromagnet

A solenoid with a soft iron core. The core greatly increases the field strength by concentrating the flux. Strength increased by: more current, more turns, or a soft iron core. Used in: relays, circuit breakers, MRI scanners, scrap metal cranes, doorbells, electric motors.

Force on a Current-Carrying Wire
F = B I l
F = force (N) · B = magnetic flux density (T) · I = current (A) · l = length of wire in the field (m)
Worked Example 1 — Force direction

Current flows East in a wire; field points North (horizontally). Find direction of force.

Left hand: middle finger → East (current); index → North (field).
Thumb points → Upward
Worked Example 2 — Magnitude of force

A 0.20 m wire carries 3.0 A at right angles to a 0.50 T field. Find F.

F = BIl = 0.50 × 3.0 × 0.20 = 0.30 N
Worked Example 3 — Solenoid polarity

Viewed from the left end of a solenoid, current flows anticlockwise. Which pole is the left end?

Anticlockwise at left end → current comes toward you when viewed from outside.
Right-hand rule: curl fingers anticlockwise from outside → thumb points away from you → left end is South pole.
Worked Example 4 — Loudspeaker

Explain how a loudspeaker uses the motor effect to produce sound.

An alternating current flows through a coil in a permanent magnetic field.
The motor effect creates a varying force on the coil → the attached cone vibrates in and out.
The vibrating cone creates compressions and rarefactions in the air → sound waves.

DC Motor and Electromagnetic Induction

§4.5.1–4.5.2

📖 Lesson Notes

The DC Electric Motor

A current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a turning force (torque). Key parts:

  • Rectangular coil (armature): carries current; placed in the magnetic field.
  • Permanent magnets: provide the external magnetic field.
  • Split-ring commutator: two copper semicircles; reverses current in the coil every half turn so the force always acts in the same rotational direction.
  • Carbon brushes: stationary contacts that press against the commutator and feed current into the rotating coil.

Motor power increased by: more current, more turns, stronger magnets, soft iron core in coil.

Electromagnetic Induction (Faraday's Law)

An EMF is induced in a conductor whenever it moves relative to a magnetic field (or whenever the magnetic flux linkage through a coil changes). Discovered by Faraday (1831). No relative motion → no induced EMF. The induced EMF increases with: faster motion, stronger field, more turns.

Lenz's Law: the direction of the induced EMF opposes the change that caused it (energy conservation).

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Dynamo Rule): thumb = Motion of wire; index = magnetic Field; middle = induced Current direction.

Worked Example 1 — Commutator purpose

Explain why the DC motor stops rotating without the split-ring commutator.

Without commutator, the current direction in the coil stays fixed as it rotates.
After half a turn, the force reverses direction → coil would oscillate, not rotate continuously.
Commutator swaps current every half turn → force always in same rotational direction → continuous rotation.
Worked Example 2 — Induction experiment

A bar magnet is pushed into a 600-turn coil connected to a galvanometer. State what happens when (a) magnet moves in, (b) stationary, (c) moves out.

(a) Moving in: galvanometer deflects (induced current).
(b) Stationary: zero deflection — no change in flux, no EMF.
(c) Moving out: galvanometer deflects in opposite direction.
Worked Example 3 — Factors affecting induced EMF

State three ways to increase the induced EMF in a coil experiment.

(a) Move the magnet faster (increase speed of relative motion).
(b) Use a stronger magnet (increase B).
(c) Use a coil with more turns.
Worked Example 4 — Lenz's Law

A N pole approaches a coil. The induced current flows anticlockwise as seen from the magnet. Explain why this is consistent with Lenz's Law.

Anticlockwise current → top of coil becomes N pole → repels the approaching N pole.
The induced effect opposes the cause (the approach of the magnet) → Lenz's Law satisfied.
Work must be done to push the magnet in → electrical energy comes from this work → energy is conserved.

Transformers and Power Transmission

§4.5.3

📖 Lesson Notes

The Transformer

A device that changes the voltage of an AC supply. Consists of two coils (primary and secondary) wound on a soft iron core. The alternating current in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic flux in the iron core, which induces an alternating EMF in the secondary coil (mutual induction). Transformers only work with AC — DC produces no changing flux and no induced EMF.

Step-Up vs Step-Down
  • Step-up transformer: more turns on secondary than primary → V_s > V_p → increases voltage. Used at power stations (before transmission).
  • Step-down transformer: fewer turns on secondary → V_s < V_p → decreases voltage. Used at substations and appliances (household).
Transformer Equations
V_s / V_p = N_s / N_p
For an ideal transformer: V_p I_p = V_s I_s (power in = power out)
V_p = primary voltage · V_s = secondary voltage · N_p = primary turns · N_s = secondary turns · I = current
Worked Example 1 — Step-up transformer

Primary: 240 V, 1000 turns. Secondary: 4000 turns. Find secondary voltage.

V_s = V_p × N_s/N_p = 240 × 4000/1000 = 960 V
Worked Example 2 — Current in secondary

Ideal transformer: V_p = 240 V, I_p = 5 A, V_s = 12 V. Find I_s.

V_p I_p = V_s I_s → 240 × 5 = 12 × I_s
I_s = 1200/12 = 100 A
Worked Example 3 — Power transmission efficiency

Why is electrical power transmitted at high voltage (400 kV) over long distances?

P = IV, so for the same power, higher V → lower I.
Heat dissipated in cables = I²R. Lower I → much less energy lost as heat in the cables.
Transmitting at 400 kV rather than 240 V reduces current by a factor of ~1667 → power loss reduced by 1667² ≈ 2.8 million times.
Worked Example 4 — Finding turns ratio

A transformer converts 11 000 V to 230 V. Find the turns ratio N_p : N_s.

N_p/N_s = V_p/V_s = 11000/230 = 47.8 : 1
This is a step-down transformer (more primary turns than secondary).

National Grid: Power stations generate at ~25 kV. Step-up transformers raise voltage to ~400 kV for long-distance transmission. Step-down transformers at local substations reduce voltage to 11 kV, then 230 V for homes. Soft iron cores are laminated (thin insulated layers) to reduce energy loss by eddy currents. Transformers are not 100% efficient due to: resistance heating in coils, eddy currents in core, hysteresis losses.

Nuclear Model of the Atom

§5.1

📖 Lesson Notes

Structure of the Atom
  • Nucleus: tiny, dense, positively charged core. Contains protons (+ charge) and neutrons (no charge).
  • Electrons: negatively charged; orbit the nucleus at relatively large distances. In a neutral atom: number of electrons = number of protons (atomic number Z).
  • Mass of atom is almost entirely in the nucleus (protons + neutrons each ≈ 1 atomic mass unit; electrons ≈ negligible).
  • Atom is mostly empty space: nucleus occupies about 10⁻¹⁵ of the atomic volume.
Proton Number, Nucleon Number, Isotopes
  • Proton number (Z): number of protons in the nucleus. Defines the element.
  • Nucleon number (A): total number of protons + neutrons = mass number.
  • Neutron number (N): N = A − Z.
  • Isotopes: atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A). Same chemical properties; different nuclear stability and mass.
  • Notation: AZX, e.g. 126C and 146C are isotopes of carbon.
Ions

If an atom loses one or more electrons → positive ion (cation). If it gains electrons → negative ion (anion). Ions have the same nuclear structure as the parent atom but different charge.

The Geiger–Marsden Alpha Scattering Experiment

Alpha particles from radium were fired at a thin gold foil. Most passed straight through (atom is mostly empty space). A small fraction was deflected at large angles. About 1 in 8000 "bounced back". Rutherford concluded (1911): all positive charge and most mass is concentrated in a tiny dense nucleus. This overturned the "plum-pudding" model.

(a) Protons = Z = 92
(b) Neutrons = A − Z = 238 − 92 = 146
(c) Electrons = Z = 92 (neutral atom)

Same: both have Z = 6 (6 protons, 6 electrons) → same element, same chemistry.

Explain the three types of alpha particle path observed in the gold foil experiment.

Path 1: straight through → most of the atom is empty space.
Path 2: slightly deflected → slight repulsion from the positive nucleus at a distance.
Path 3: backscattered (large deflection or reversed) → head-on approach to the tiny positive nucleus → strong electrostatic repulsion.
Worked Example 4 — Plum-pudding model

Why does the alpha scattering experiment disprove the plum-pudding model?

Plum-pudding model: positive charge spread throughout the atom → alpha particles should be deflected very slightly (no concentrated repulsion).
Large-angle deflections and backscattering require all positive charge to be concentrated in a very small, dense nucleus.

Radioactivity — Detection and Types

§5.2.1–5.2.2

📖 Lesson Notes

Radioactivity

The spontaneous, random emission of radiation from unstable atomic nuclei. Spontaneous: not affected by temperature, pressure, or chemical state. Random: cannot predict which nucleus will decay next or when. Discovered by Becquerel (1896); radium isolated by Marie Curie shortly after.

Background Radiation

We are all exposed to radiation from natural and artificial sources. Main natural sources:

  • Radon gas from rocks and soil (granite buildings; basements)
  • Cosmic rays from the Sun and space (higher at altitude)
  • Naturally occurring isotopes in food and drink (potassium-40)
  • Rocks and building materials

Artificial sources: medical procedures (X-rays, tracers), nuclear power stations, fallout from weapons testing. Background count must be subtracted from measurements to get the corrected count-rate for a source.

Detecting Radiation — Geiger–Müller (GM) Tube

Radiation enters through a thin mica window (or through walls for penetrating radiation) → ionises argon gas inside → ions accelerated to electrodes → produces a current pulse → registered as a "count" on a ratemeter or scaler. Count-rate = counts per second (or per minute).

The Three Types of Radiation
  • Alpha (α): helium-4 nucleus (²₄He²⁺); charge +2; mass 4 u.
  • Beta (β⁻): fast-moving electron from the nucleus (when a neutron converts to a proton); charge −1; mass negligible.
  • Gamma (γ): high-frequency electromagnetic wave emitted from the nucleus; no charge; no mass; travels at c.
Worked Example 1 — Background correction

A source gives a count-rate of 190 counts/s. Background count in 1 minute = 300. Find corrected count-rate.

Background rate = 300/60 = 5 counts/s
Corrected count-rate = 190 − 5 = 185 counts/s
Worked Example 2 — Why random?

Explain why radioactive decay is described as random.

Any individual nucleus may decay at any time; it is impossible to predict which nucleus will decay next.
The probability of any given nucleus decaying in a fixed time interval is constant.
The count-rate fluctuates unpredictably around an average value.
Worked Example 3 — Identifying radiation types

State the charge and composition of (a) an alpha particle, (b) a beta particle, (c) a gamma ray.

(a) Alpha: charge +2; 2 protons + 2 neutrons (helium nucleus).
(b) Beta: charge −1; fast electron (from nucleus when n → p + e⁻ + antineutrino).
(c) Gamma: charge 0; high-frequency EM wave from an excited nucleus.
Worked Example 4 — Source of beta particle

Explain why a beta particle is described as originating from the nucleus even though electrons orbit the nucleus.

The beta particle is not an orbital electron — it is created in the nucleus when a neutron decays: n → p + e⁻ + antineutrino.
The newly created electron is immediately emitted at high speed from the nucleus.

Properties of α, β and γ Radiation

§5.2.2

📖 Lesson Notes

Penetrating Power and Absorption
  • Alpha (α): very low penetrating power. Stopped by: a few cm of air, or a single sheet of paper, or the dead outer layer of skin. Cannot penetrate into the body from outside.
  • Beta (β): moderate penetrating power. Stopped by: a few mm of aluminium (a few metres of air).
  • Gamma (γ): high penetrating power. Reduced (but not fully stopped) by several cm of lead or several metres of concrete. Intensity decreases exponentially with absorber thickness.
Ionising Ability
  • Alpha: most strongly ionising (charge +2, large, slow-moving → many ionising collisions per cm). Most dangerous if inhaled or ingested.
  • Beta: moderately ionising (smaller, faster, charge −1).
  • Gamma: weakly ionising (no charge → fewer direct ionising interactions). Most penetrating → dangerous externally.
Deflection in Fields
  • Alpha: deflected by electric and magnetic fields (charge +2). Deflects opposite to beta.
  • Beta: deflected by electric and magnetic fields (charge −1). Deflects more than alpha (lighter).
  • Gamma: not deflected (no charge).

Radioactive Decay Equations

§5.2.3

📖 Lesson Notes

Nuclear Decay Equations

In all nuclear equations, the total nucleon number (A) and the total proton number (Z) are conserved on both sides. This is the fundamental rule for writing and balancing decay equations.

Alpha Decay

The nucleus emits a 42He nucleus (alpha particle). The parent nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons. The daughter nucleus has: A decreases by 4; Z decreases by 2. The daughter element is two places to the left in the periodic table.

Beta-Minus Decay (β⁻)

A neutron in the nucleus converts to a proton: n → p + e⁻ + antineutrino. The nucleus emits a fast electron (0−1e). The daughter nucleus has: A unchanged; Z increases by 1. The daughter element is one place to the right in the periodic table.

Gamma Emission

Follows alpha or beta decay when the daughter nucleus is in an excited state. A gamma photon is emitted; A and Z are unchanged — only energy is lost. Gamma emission does not change the element.

Nuclear Decay Summary
Alpha: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z−2) Y + ⁴_2 He
Beta (β⁻): ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + ⁰_(-1) e
Gamma: ᴬ_Z X* → ᴬ_Z X + γ (A and Z unchanged)
Conservation rules: sum of A on left = sum of A on right; sum of Z on left = sum of Z on right
Worked Example 1 — Alpha decay of uranium

Uranium-238 (²³⁸₉₂U) undergoes alpha decay. Write the equation and identify the daughter nucleus.

²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Y + ⁴₂He
Daughter: A = 238−4 = 234; Z = 92−2 = 90 → Thorium-234 (²³⁴₉₀Th)
Worked Example 2 — Beta decay of carbon-14

Carbon-14 (¹⁴₆C) undergoes beta-minus decay. Write the equation.

¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇Y + ⁰₋₁e
Daughter: A = 14 (unchanged); Z = 6+1 = 7 → Nitrogen-14 (¹⁴₇N)
Worked Example 3 — Completing an equation

²²⁶₈₈Ra → ²²²₈₆X + ⁴₂He. Identify element X.

Z = 88−2 = 86 → Radon (Rn); A = 226−4 = 222
X = ²²²₈₆Rn (Radon-222)
Worked Example 4 — Gamma and combined decay

Radon-222 undergoes alpha decay followed by gamma emission. Write both equations.

Alpha: ²²²₈₆Rn → ²¹⁸₈₄Po + ⁴₂He
Gamma: ²¹⁸₈₄Po* → ²¹⁸₈₄Po + γ  (A and Z unchanged)

The conservation laws mean we can always find the unknown daughter nucleus in a decay equation by subtraction. These laws also confirm that atoms of one element are transformed into atoms of a different element during alpha or beta decay — a process called transmutation. This is why nuclear reactions cannot be reversed by chemical means.

Half-Life

§5.2.4

📖 Lesson Notes

Half-Life (t½)

The time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei (or the activity/count-rate) to fall to half of its initial value. Half-life is a constant property of each radioactive isotope — it is not affected by temperature, pressure, or chemical state. It can range from fractions of a second (some artificial isotopes) to billions of years (uranium-238: 4.5 × 10⁹ years).

Activity and Count-Rate Decay

Activity = number of decays per second; unit: becquerel (Bq). After each half-life, the activity halves. The number of undecayed nuclei also halves. This produces an exponential decay curve: starts steep, then levels off, never quite reaching zero.

Half-Life Calculations
After n half-lives: N = N₀ × (1/2)ⁿ
A = A₀ × (1/2)ⁿ  (activity)
Total time elapsed = n × t½
N = remaining nuclei · A = activity · N₀, A₀ = initial values · n = number of half-lives
Worked Example 1 — Simple half-life decay

A source has initial activity 1600 Bq and half-life 4 days. Find activity after 12 days.

n = 12/4 = 3 half-lives
A = 1600 × (1/2)³ = 1600/8 = 200 Bq
Worked Example 2 — Finding half-life from decay curve

A decay graph shows count-rate drops from 800 counts/min to 400 counts/min in 30 minutes, then to 200 in a further 30 minutes. Find half-life.

The count-rate halves every 30 minutes.
Half-life = 30 minutes
Worked Example 3 — Number of nuclei remaining

A sample starts with 6.4 × 10¹⁰ nuclei and has t½ = 2 hours. How many remain after 10 hours?

n = 10/2 = 5 half-lives
N = 6.4 × 10¹⁰ × (1/2)⁵ = 6.4 × 10¹⁰ / 32 = 2.0 × 10⁹ nuclei
Worked Example 4 — Time elapsed

The activity of a sample falls from 960 Bq to 60 Bq. The half-life is 3 hours. Find the time elapsed.

960 → 480 → 240 → 120 → 60 (count the halvings: 4 half-lives)
Time = 4 × 3 = 12 hours

Radiocarbon dating: Carbon-14 (¹⁴C) is produced continuously in the atmosphere by cosmic ray bombardment of nitrogen. All living organisms absorb ¹⁴C in proportion to its atmospheric level. When an organism dies, it stops absorbing ¹⁴C, and the existing ¹⁴C decays with t½ = 5730 years. Measuring the current ¹⁴C activity and comparing with the original level allows the age of organic material to be estimated (up to ~50 000 years).

Uses of Radioactivity and Radiation Safety
Term 3 · Nuclear Physics and Space

📖 Lesson Notes

Radioactive isotopes have valuable applications in medicine, industry, and archaeology. However, ionising radiation is hazardous, and careful safety protocols must be followed at all times.

Tracer (medical) A radioactive isotope introduced into the body whose movement can be tracked externally using a gamma detector. Beta or gamma emitters are used (not alpha) because they can penetrate tissue and be detected outside the body.

Medical Applications

Tracers in diagnosis: Technetium-99m (⁹⁹ᵐTc) is the most widely used medical tracer. It is a gamma emitter with a short half-life of about 6 hours — long enough to complete scanning, short enough to minimise dose to the patient. It can be attached to molecules that target specific organs (thyroid, kidney, bone).

Iodine-131: Used to diagnose and treat thyroid problems. The thyroid naturally concentrates iodine, so radioactive iodine accumulates there. High doses destroy overactive thyroid tissue (radiotherapy).

Radiotherapy (cancer treatment): A beam of gamma radiation is directed at a tumour from multiple angles so the tumour receives a high dose while surrounding healthy tissue receives a lower dose. Cobalt-60 (gamma emitter) is used in gamma knife surgery for brain tumours.

Sterilisation: Medical instruments and bandages are exposed to intense gamma radiation from cobalt-60, which kills bacteria and viruses. No chemicals are needed and sealed packaging is unaffected.

Industrial Applications

Thickness gauging: A radioactive source and a detector are placed on opposite sides of a sheet (paper, metal, plastic). If the sheet is too thick, less radiation reaches the detector; the output adjusts the rollers. Beta emitters are used for thin sheets (paper); gamma emitters for thick metal sheets.

Weld inspection (gamma radiography): A gamma source is placed on one side of a weld and photographic film on the other. Cracks or voids absorb less radiation and appear as dark patches on the film — like an X-ray of the weld.

Smoke detectors: Americium-241 (alpha emitter) ionises the air between two plates, producing a small current. Smoke particles absorb the alpha particles, the current drops, and the alarm sounds. Alpha is used because it is easily absorbed — it won't escape the detector casing to harm users.

Geological and Archaeological Dating

Radiocarbon dating (¹⁴C): Used for organic materials (wood, bone, charcoal). t½ = 5730 years. Reliable up to ~50 000 years (after ~8–9 half-lives the activity becomes too small to measure accurately).

Uranium–lead dating: ²³⁸U decays through a series of steps to ²⁰⁶Pb with an effective half-life of 4.5 billion years. The ratio of ²³⁸U to ²⁰⁶Pb in a rock gives its age. Used for rocks billions of years old.

Radiation Safety

The ALARA principle states that radiation exposure should be As Low As Reasonably Achievable. Three main protection measures:

Distance Radiation intensity follows an inverse square law: doubling the distance reduces intensity by a factor of 4. Always use tongs or remote manipulators — never handle sources directly.
Shielding Alpha: paper or a few cm of air. Beta: a few mm of aluminium. Gamma: several cm of lead or thick concrete.
Time Minimise the time spent near a radioactive source. Total dose = dose rate × time.

Other precautions: Wear lead aprons and gloves when handling gamma sources. Use dosimeters or film badges to monitor cumulative exposure. Never eat, drink, or put hands near mouth in a radiation lab. Store sources in lead-lined containers with warning labels. Point sources away from the body. Wash hands after use.

Worked Example 1 — Choosing the correct isotope

Explain why technetium-99m (t½ = 6 hours, gamma emitter) is preferred over iodine-131 (t½ = 8 days, beta/gamma emitter) for a kidney scan.

Technetium-99m is a pure gamma emitter — gamma rays penetrate tissue and can be detected outside the body.
Its short half-life (6 h) means the patient's exposure is brief: after ~5 half-lives (30 h), less than 4% of the original activity remains.
Iodine-131's 8-day half-life would expose the patient to significant radiation for a much longer period — unsuitable unless treatment (not just diagnosis) is the goal.
Worked Example 2 — Smoke detector principle

State why an alpha source is used in a smoke detector rather than a beta or gamma source.

Alpha radiation has a high ionising power, which efficiently ionises the air between the plates to create the detection current.
Alpha radiation has a very short range in air (~5 cm) and is stopped by the plastic casing of the detector — it poses no external radiation hazard to people in the room.
Beta or gamma sources would penetrate the casing and irradiate occupants unnecessarily.
Worked Example 3 — Thickness gauging source choice

A factory uses a radioactive source to monitor the thickness of aluminium sheets (each ~2 mm thick). Suggest the most appropriate type of radiation and justify your answer.

Use a beta source.
Beta particles have a range of a few mm in aluminium — they are partially absorbed by a 2 mm sheet, so the transmitted count-rate is sensitive to small changes in thickness.
Alpha would be completely stopped by the sheet. Gamma would pass through almost unaffected by small thickness changes — neither would be sensitive enough.
Worked Example 4 — Radiation protection calculation

A worker 1 m from a source receives a dose rate of 20 mSv/h. At what distance would the dose rate fall to 1.25 mSv/h?

Intensity ∝ 1/d² → I₁d₁² = I₂d₂²
20 × 1² = 1.25 × d₂²
d₂² = 20/1.25 = 16 → d₂ = 4 m

The Earth and Solar System
Term 3 · Nuclear Physics and Space

📖 Lesson Notes

Our Solar System consists of the Sun — a medium-sized star — orbited by eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, comets, and asteroids. Understanding orbital mechanics explains day and night, seasons, and the motion of satellites.

Day and Night

Earth rotates on its own axis once every 24 hours (one day). The half of Earth facing the Sun experiences day; the half facing away experiences night. The Sun rises in the east and sets in the west because Earth rotates from west to east.

The Year and Seasons

Earth orbits the Sun once every 365.25 days (one year). Earth's axis is tilted at 23.5° to its orbital plane. This tilt causes seasons:

  • When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun, the Sun is higher in the sky, days are longer, and temperatures are higher → summer in the Northern Hemisphere.
  • Six months later, the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun → winter in the Northern Hemisphere.
  • The Southern Hemisphere experiences the opposite seasons simultaneously.

Seasons are caused by the axial tilt, not by changes in distance from the Sun (Earth's orbit is nearly circular).

The Moon

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite. It orbits Earth approximately every 28 days. The Moon does not produce its own light — we see it because it reflects sunlight. As the Moon orbits Earth, we see different fractions of its illuminated face → the lunar phases (new moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, full moon).

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun, casting a shadow on Earth. A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth passes between the Sun and the Moon.

The asteroid belt lies between Mars and Jupiter. Comets are icy bodies on highly elliptical orbits; their tails always point away from the Sun (blown by the solar wind).

Orbital Speed

v = 2πr / T

Where v = orbital speed (m/s), r = orbital radius (m), T = orbital period (s). Planets closer to the Sun have smaller r but much shorter T, resulting in higher orbital speeds (Kepler's laws).

Satellites

Natural satellites are moons. Artificial satellites are human-made objects placed in orbit.

Geostationary orbit Altitude ≈ 36 000 km above Earth's equator. Orbital period = 24 hours, so the satellite appears stationary above a fixed point on Earth. Used for TV broadcasting, weather observation, and communications.
Polar (low Earth) orbit Altitude ~200–2000 km. Orbital period ~90–120 minutes. The satellite passes over different regions as Earth rotates beneath it, eventually covering the whole surface. Used for Earth observation, GPS, and spy satellites.

Explain why it is hotter in summer than in winter in the Northern Hemisphere. Your answer must refer to the Earth's axial tilt.

Stars and Stellar Life Cycles
Term 3 · Nuclear Physics and Space

📖 Lesson Notes

Stars are enormous, self-luminous balls of plasma held together by gravity and powered by nuclear fusion. Their mass at birth largely determines their life story — low-mass stars like our Sun live billions of years and end quietly; high-mass stars burn fast and die violently.

The Sun

The Sun is a medium-sized star composed mainly of hydrogen (~73%) and helium (~25%) with trace amounts of heavier elements. Its surface temperature is about 5500 °C (photosphere), rising to ~15 million °C at the core. Energy is released by nuclear fusion: hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium nuclei, releasing enormous amounts of energy according to E = mc².

The Sun is approximately 4.6 billion years old and is roughly halfway through its main-sequence lifetime.

The Light-Year

Light-year (ly) The distance light travels in one year. 1 ly = 9.5 × 10¹⁵ m ≈ 9.5 × 10¹² km. It is a unit of distance, not time.

The nearest star (Proxima Centauri) is ~4.2 light-years away. The Milky Way galaxy is ~100 000 light-years in diameter. When we observe distant stars, we see them as they were when the light left them — we are looking back in time.

Stellar Life Cycle — Low-Mass Stars (like the Sun)

Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence Star → Red Giant → Planetary Nebula → White Dwarf
  • Nebula: Cloud of hydrogen gas and dust. Gravity causes it to contract.
  • Protostar: Contracting cloud heats up. Nuclear fusion has not yet started.
  • Main sequence star: Core temperature sufficient for hydrogen fusion. Radiation pressure outward balances gravity inward — hydrostatic equilibrium. This is the longest stage (Sun: ~10 billion years total).
  • Red giant: Hydrogen in core is exhausted. Core contracts and heats further; outer layers expand enormously and cool → red colour. Helium fusion begins in the core.
  • Planetary nebula: Outer layers are expelled gently as a glowing shell of gas.
  • White dwarf: The exposed core — dense, hot, and no longer fusing. About Earth's size but Sun's mass. Cools slowly over billions of years → eventually a dark, cold black dwarf.

Stellar Life Cycle — High-Mass Stars

Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence Star → Red Supergiant → Supernova → Neutron Star or Black Hole
  • Red supergiant: Massive stars expand to become much larger and more luminous than red giants.
  • Supernova: The core collapses catastrophically then rebounds in an enormous explosion — briefly outshining an entire galaxy. Heavy elements are forged and scattered into space. This is the origin of elements heavier than iron in the universe.
  • Neutron star: The remaining core of a supernova for stars with core mass ~1.4–3 solar masses. Extremely dense — a teaspoon would weigh ~10⁸ tonnes. Some neutron stars are observed as rapidly spinning pulsars.
  • Black hole: If the core mass exceeds ~3 solar masses, gravity overwhelms all other forces and collapse continues without limit. Gravity is so extreme that even light cannot escape from inside the event horizon.

Nuclear Fusion in Stars

In the core of a main-sequence star: 4 ¹H → ¹⁴He + energy (the proton–proton chain). The products have slightly less mass than the reactants; this mass difference is converted to energy (E = mc²). This process releases approximately 3.8 × 10²⁶ W from the Sun.

Worked Example 1 — Life cycle comparison

Compare the fate of a star like the Sun with that of a star 20 times the Sun's mass. Include the stages after the main sequence.

Sun-like star: after main sequence → red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf (gradual, quiet end).
20× mass: after main sequence → red supergiant → supernova (violent explosion) → likely black hole (mass too great for neutron star to be stable).
The key difference: higher mass means shorter main-sequence lifetime, more extreme energy release, and a violent end.

Worked Example 3 — Hydrostatic equilibrium

Explain why a main-sequence star maintains a stable size for billions of years.

Gravity pulls all the star's mass inward, tending to compress the star.
Nuclear fusion in the core releases energy, producing a radiation pressure (and thermal pressure) that pushes outward.
These two forces balance — hydrostatic equilibrium — keeping the star at a stable size for as long as hydrogen fuel lasts.
Worked Example 4 — Origin of elements

The planet Earth contains large amounts of iron and silicon. Explain the stellar origin of these elements.

Elements up to iron are formed by nuclear fusion reactions in the cores of massive stars during their main-sequence and supergiant stages.
Elements heavier than iron (and additional iron/silicon) are forged in supernova explosions when conditions of extreme temperature and neutron flux allow rapid neutron capture.
The supernova scatters these elements into space where they form new nebulae. Our Solar System — and everything in it including the Earth — formed from the debris of earlier stellar generations.

The Universe, Galaxies and the Big Bang
Term 3 · Nuclear Physics and Space

📖 Lesson Notes

The Universe contains hundreds of billions of galaxies, each containing hundreds of billions of stars. Understanding how the Universe began and how it is evolving is one of the greatest achievements of modern physics.

Galaxies

Galaxy A gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. Galaxies contain between millions and hundreds of billions of stars. They come in spiral, elliptical, and irregular forms.

Our galaxy is the Milky Way — a spiral galaxy about 100 000 light-years in diameter containing roughly 200–400 billion stars. The Sun is located about 26 000 light-years from the galactic centre, in one of the spiral arms. The nearest large galaxy is the Andromeda galaxy (~2.5 million light-years away).

Red-Shift

Red-shift The increase in wavelength (shift towards the red end of the spectrum) of light from a source moving away from the observer. The greater the red-shift, the faster the source is receding.

When we analyse the light from distant galaxies, all their spectral lines are shifted to longer wavelengths compared to laboratory sources. This indicates that all galaxies are moving away from us. Furthermore, the more distant the galaxy, the greater its red-shift — and hence the faster it recedes.

This observation is the Doppler effect applied to light. Just as the pitch of a police siren drops as it moves away, the frequency of light from a receding source decreases (wavelength increases).

Hubble's Law

v ∝ d    (recession speed is proportional to distance)

Edwin Hubble (1929) showed that the recession speed of a galaxy is proportional to its distance from us. This is expressed as Hubble's Law: v = H₀d, where H₀ is the Hubble constant (~70 km/s per Megaparsec). The implication is profound: the Universe is expanding uniformly in all directions.

The Big Bang Theory

If all galaxies are currently moving apart, then in the past they must have been closer together. Extrapolating backwards leads to the conclusion that all matter, energy, space, and time originated from an extremely hot, dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago. This event is called the Big Bang.

The Big Bang was not an explosion into space — it was an expansion of space itself. There is no "centre" of the explosion; every point in the Universe sees every other point moving away from it.

Evidence for the Big Bang

1. Red-shift of galaxies: All distant galaxies show red-shift. The more distant they are, the greater the red-shift (Hubble's Law). This shows the Universe is expanding, consistent with a Big Bang origin.

2. Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation: The Universe should still be filled with thermal radiation left over from the Big Bang, now cooled to just ~2.7 K above absolute zero. This was predicted theoretically and discovered by Penzias and Wilson in 1965. It arrives from all directions in the sky with almost uniform intensity — exactly as predicted. This is the strongest evidence for the Big Bang model.

The Steady State Theory

Before the Big Bang was accepted, an alternative — the Steady State model — proposed that the Universe has always existed in a constant state; new matter is continuously created as the Universe expands, keeping its density constant. The Steady State theory cannot explain the CMB radiation and has been abandoned by mainstream science.

The Future of the Universe

Whether the expansion of the Universe will continue forever or eventually reverse depends on the total mass-energy content. Current observations suggest the expansion is actually accelerating, driven by a mysterious "dark energy." The far-future fate of the Universe remains an open question in cosmology.

Worked Example 1 — Interpreting red-shift

The hydrogen alpha line has a rest wavelength of 656 nm. In the spectrum of a distant galaxy, this line appears at 689 nm. (a) Calculate the fractional red-shift. (b) Is this galaxy moving towards or away from us?

(a) Δλ = 689 − 656 = 33 nm; fractional red-shift = Δλ/λ = 33/656 = 0.050 (5.0%)
(b) The wavelength has increased (shifted to red) → the galaxy is moving away from us.
Worked Example 2 — Hubble's Law

Galaxy A is 100 Mpc away and has a recession speed of 7000 km/s. Galaxy B is 250 Mpc away. Using Hubble's Law, estimate the recession speed of Galaxy B.

H₀ = v/d = 7000 / 100 = 70 km/s/Mpc
v_B = H₀ × d_B = 70 × 250 = 17 500 km/s
Worked Example 3 — Evidence for the Big Bang

State two pieces of evidence that support the Big Bang theory and explain briefly how each provides support.

Red-shift: All distant galaxies have red-shifted spectra. Greater distance → greater red-shift → greater recession speed. This shows the Universe is expanding, which implies it originated from a single dense point.
CMB radiation: A faint, uniform microwave radiation fills the Universe in all directions at ~2.7 K. This matches the predicted thermal afterglow of a hot, dense early Universe that has since cooled for 13.8 billion years — it cannot be explained by the Steady State model.
Worked Example 4 — Why the Steady State theory was rejected

Explain why the Steady State theory of the Universe is no longer accepted.

The Steady State theory predicted a Universe that looks the same at all times and in all directions — it does not predict any thermal background radiation from a hot early phase.
The discovery of the CMB (a uniform microwave background at 2.7 K coming from all directions) was predicted by the Big Bang theory but is completely unexplained by the Steady State theory.
Additionally, observations of distant galaxies show they looked different in the past (more quasars, different morphologies), meaning the Universe is not the same at all times — contradicting the Steady State model.
